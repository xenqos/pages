## Instructions
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Build the 555 oscillator circuit shown in Figures 1 and 2.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/CMOS-555-long-duration-red-LED-flasher-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 2. </strong>Schematic diagram of the 555 timer LED flasher with transistor current drive.</em>

<p>Omit the red jumper wire shown at the upper left of Figure 1. The LED&nbsp;should start flashing roughly every 1 second.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>Measure the forward voltage of the LED, V<sub>F</sub>, by adding the jumper shown in red in Figure 1. Adding this jumper&nbsp;will turn the LED on full-time. Use a voltmeter to measure the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drop</a>&nbsp;across the LED while it is turned on.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Calculate the approximate current through the LED using the following equation:</p>

<p>$$I_{LED} \approx \frac{( V_{DD} - V_F)}{R4}$$</p>

<p>LEDs can have a lot of variation in the forward voltage. Using that equation with our supply voltage of 3.0 V and an R4 of 27 &Omega;:</p>
<ul>
<li>If V<sub>F</sub> = 2.5 V, the LED current would be 18.5 mA</li>
<li>If V<sub>F</sub>&nbsp;= 2.1 V, the LED current would be 33.3 mA</li>
<li>If V<sub>F</sub>&nbsp;= 1.5 V, the LED current would be 55.6 mA</li>
</ul>

<p>A forward current of 55.6 mA might cause the LED to burn out. Not to mention it would negatively impact the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/tools/battery-lifetime-calculator/" target="_blank">battery life</a> of the system. If we want to target an LED forward current of 20 mA (a typical value), we can calculate values for R4 using this equation:</p>

<p>$$R4 = \frac{( V_{DD} - V_F)}{I_{LED}} = \frac{( 3.0 - V_F)}{0.02}$$</p>

<p>Using that equation, we can calculate the following resistance values for R4:</p>
<ul>
<li>If V<sub>F</sub> = 2.5 V, use a 25&nbsp;&Omega; resistor&nbsp;</li>
<li>If V<sub>F</sub> = 2.1 V, use a 45&nbsp;&Omega; resistor&nbsp;</li>
<li>If V<sub>F&nbsp;</sub>= 1.5 V, use a 75&nbsp;&Omega; resistor&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>It was mentioned in the previous experiment that capacitor, C2, extended the life of the batteries. Try removing this capacitor&nbsp;and see what happens. After a time, you&nbsp;will begin to notice a dimming of the LED. After a week or two, the circuit will die without the capacitor. Now, place the capacitor back into the circuit. The circuit will resume working in a couple of seconds. The capacitor provides the surge current required when the LED turns on.&nbsp;This flasher will work for 3 months using fresh alkaline AAA batteries.</p>

## Theory of Operation
<p>The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/digital/chpt-3/cmos-gate-circuitry/" target="_blank">complimentary metal-oxide semiconductor (CMOS)</a>&nbsp;555 oscillator was explained fully in the previous <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-8/cmos-555-long-duration-minimum-parts-red-led-flasher/" target="_blank">555 Lab - Red LED Flasher</a> experiment, so the transistor driver will be the focus of this explanation.&nbsp;The transistors, Q1 and Q2, used to drive the LED&nbsp;are non critical. They&nbsp;are&nbsp;designed to minimize the load on the TLC555 IC, while still turning&nbsp;Q2 on fully. This is important because&nbsp;as the battery voltage approaches 2 V, the drive from the TLC555 is reduced to its minimum value and might otherwise not be able directly drive an LED. <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-4/bipolar-junction-transistors-bjt/" target="_blank">Bipolar transistors</a>&nbsp;make excellent switches in thes types of applications.</p>
<p>The transistor driver combines elements of a common collector configuration on Q1, along with the common emitter configuration of Q2. This allows for very high input resistance while allowing Q2 to turn on fully.</p>
<p>The input resistance of a transistor is the &beta; (gain) of the transistor times the emitter resistor. If Q1 has a gain of 50 (a minimum value) then the driver loads the TLC555 with more than 100 K&Omega;. This provides a high <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-5/review-of-r-x-and-z/" target="_blank">impedance</a> load to the 555 output, so it does not need to drive much current. Transistors can have large variations in gain, even within the same family.</p>
<p>When Q1 turns on, 1 mA is passed to Q2. This is more than enough to turn Q2 fully on, which is referred to as saturation. Q2 is used as a simple switch for the LED.</p>
