<h1>Si Lab - Half-wave Rectifier</h1>
<h3>Project Overview</h3>
<p>In this project, you will build and test the half-wave <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/rectifier-circuits/">rectifying circuit</a>, illustrated in Figure 1.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/half-wave_rectifier_figure_1_redraw.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1.</strong> Half-wave rectifier circuit for motor drive.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/rectifier-circuits/" target="_blank">Half-wave rectifiers</a> are basic circuits for converting AC (alternative currents) <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltages</a> to pseudo-DC (direct current).&nbsp;They rely on the primarily one-way conduction of semiconductor <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/diode-characteristics-circuits/" target="_blank">diodes</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Parts and Materials</h3>
<ul>
<li>Low-voltage AC power supply (6 VAC output)</li>
<li>6 V battery</li>
<li><a href="https://www.vishay.com/docs/88503/1n4001.pdf" target="_blank">1N4001</a> rectifying diode or any of the 1N400X series of rectifying diodes</li>
<li>Small hobby&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-13/introduction-ac-motors/" target="_blank">motor</a>, permanent-magnet type</li>
<li>Optional:&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/sensitive-audio-detector/" target="_blank">audio detector</a> with headphones, built in the AC Lab&nbsp;projects</li>
<li>Optional: 0.1 &micro;F <a href="https://eepower.com/capacitor-guide/" target="_blank">capacitor</a></li>
</ul>
<p>A 0.1 &micro;F capacitor is specified for coupling&nbsp;the audio detector to the circuit so that only AC reaches the detector circuit.&nbsp;This capacitor&rsquo;s value is not critical. I&rsquo;ve used capacitors ranging from 0.27 &micro;F to 0.015 &micro;F with success. Lower capacitor values attenuate low-frequency signals to a greater degree, resulting in less sound intensity from the headphones, so use a greater&nbsp;capacitor value if you experience difficulty hearing the tones.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Learning Objectives</h3>
<ul>
<li>The function of a diode as a rectifier</li>
<li>Permanent magnet motor operation on AC versus DC power</li>
<li>Measuring ripple&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-2/voltage-usage/" target="_blank">voltage with a voltmeter</a></li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Instructions</h3>
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Connect the motor to the low-voltage AC power supply through the rectifying diode, as seen in the illustration of Figure 1 and the schematic diagram of Figure 2.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/half-wave-rectifier-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 2.</strong>&nbsp;A half-wave rectifier circuit schematic.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The diode&nbsp;only allows current to pass through during the positive&nbsp;half-cycle of a full positive-and-negative cycle of power supply voltage, eliminating the negative half-cycle from ever reaching the motor.&nbsp;As a result, the motor only sees&nbsp;current in one direction, albeit a pulsating current, allowing it to spin in one direction.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>Take a jumper wire and short past the diode momentarily, as illustrated in Figure 3, noting the effect on the motor&rsquo;s operation.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/half-wave_rectifier_figure_3.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 3.</strong> Bypassing the diode&nbsp;rectifier to drive the motor with AC.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>As you should see, permanent-magnet DC&nbsp;motors do not function well on <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-1/what-is-alternating-current-ac/" target="_blank">alternating current</a>&nbsp;(AC).</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Remove the temporary jumper wire and reverse the diode&rsquo;s orientation in the circuit. Note the effect on the motor.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4: </strong>Return the diode to its original orientation, as shown in Figures 1 and 2. Measure the DC voltage and then the AC voltage across the motor, as illustrated in Figure 4.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/half-wave_rectifier_figure_4.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 4.</strong> Measuring the voltage at the motor (shown with the multimeter set to measure AC voltage).</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Most digital <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-8/multimeters/" target="_blank">multimeters</a> do a good job of discriminating AC from DC voltage.&nbsp;These two measurements show the DC average and AC ripple&nbsp;voltages, respectively, of the power seen&nbsp;by the motor.</p>
<p>Ripple voltage is the varying portion of the voltage, interpreted as an AC quantity by measurement equipment, although the voltage waveform never actually reverses polarity.&nbsp;Ripple may be envisioned as an AC signal superimposed on a steady DC bias&nbsp;or offset&nbsp;signal.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Compare these measurements of DC and AC with voltage measurements taken across the motor while powered by a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-11/battery-construction/" target="_blank">battery</a>,&nbsp;as shown in Figure 5.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/half-wave-rectifier-illustration-5.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 5.</strong>&nbsp;Measuring the voltage at the motor when powered by a&nbsp;battery.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Batteries provide pure&nbsp;DC power, and as a result, there should be little AC voltage measured across the motor in this circuit.</p>
<p>Whatever AC voltage is measured across the motor is due to the motor&rsquo;s pulsating current draw as the brushes make and break contact with the rotating commutator bars. This pulsating current causes pulsating voltages to be dropped across any stray resistances in the circuit, resulting in pulsating voltage dips&nbsp;at the motor terminals.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6 (Optional):&nbsp;</strong>A qualitative assessment of ripple voltage may be obtained by using the sensitive audio detector described in the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/introduction-to-ac-circuits/" target="_blank">AC Lab experiments chapter</a>. This is the same device described as a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-3/sensitive-voltage-detector/" target="_blank">sensitive voltage detector</a>&nbsp;in the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-3/introduction-to-dc-circuits/" target="_blank">DC Lab experiments chapter</a>.&nbsp;Turn the detector&rsquo;s sensitivity down for low volume, and connect it across the motor terminals through a small (0.1 &micro;F) <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-13/practical-considerations-capacitors/" target="_blank">capacitor</a>, as shown in Figure 6.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/half-wave-rectifier-illustration-6.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 6.</strong>&nbsp;Connecting the sensitive audio detector to the battery-powered&nbsp;motor driver circuit.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>With a battery powering the motor, the ripple should sound like a high-pitched buzz&nbsp;or whine.</p>
<p>The capacitor acts as a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-8/high-pass-filters/" target="_blank">high-pass filter</a>, blocking DC voltage from reaching the detector and allowing easier listening to&nbsp;the remaining AC voltage. This is the exact same technique used in <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/worksheets/basic-oscilloscope-operation/" target="_blank">oscilloscope</a> circuitry for AC coupling,&nbsp;where DC signals are blocked from viewing by a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-series-circuits/" target="_blank">series-connected</a> capacitor.</p>
<p><strong>Step 7 (Optional):&nbsp;</strong>Try replacing the battery with the AC power supply and rectifying the diode, listening&nbsp;with the detector to the low-pitched buzz&nbsp;of the half-wave rectified power (Figure 7):</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/half-wave_rectifier_figure_7.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 7.</strong>&nbsp;Connecting the sensitive audio detector to the half-wave rectifier motor driver circuit.</em></h5>

<h3>SPICE Simulation of the Half-wave Rectifier Circuit</h3>
<p>We can simulate the half-wave rectifier circuit by adding <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/reference/chpt-7/introduction-to-spice/" target="_blank">SPICE</a> node numbers, as illustrated in Figure 8.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/schematic-with-spice-node-numbers_1.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 8.</strong>&nbsp;SPICE circuit schematic for a half-wave rectifier circuit.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Netlist (make a text file containing the following text, verbatim):</p>
<pre>
Halfwave rectifier &#10;v1 1 0 sin(0 8.485 60 0 0) &#10;rload 2 0 10k &#10;d1 1 2 mod1 &#10;.model mod1 d &#10;.tran .5m 25m &#10;.plot tran v(1,0) v(2,0) &#10;.end </pre>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This simulation plots the input voltage as a sine wave and the output voltage as a series of humps&nbsp;corresponding to the positive half-cycles of the AC source voltage. The dynamics of a DC motor are far too complex to be simulated using SPICE, unfortunately.</p>
<p>The AC source voltage is specified as 8.485 V instead of 6 V because SPICE&nbsp;understands AC voltage in terms of <em>peak</em> value only. A 6 V <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/how-standard-deviations-relates-rms-values/" target="_blank">root mean square (RMS)</a> sine-wave voltage is actually an 8.485 V peak. In simulations where the distinction between RMS and the peak value isn&rsquo;t relevant, I will not bother with an RMS-to-peak conversion like this.&nbsp;To be truthful, the distinction is not terribly important in this simulation, but I discuss it here for your edification.</p>
