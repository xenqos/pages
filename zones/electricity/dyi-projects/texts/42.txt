<h3>Instructions</h3>
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Connect each speaker to the low-voltage AC <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-9/power-supply-circuits/" target="_blank">power supply</a> through a 220 &Omega; resistor, as illustrated in Figures 1 and 2.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/sound-cancellation-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Schematic diagram of the noise cancellation project</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/resistors/" target="_blank">resistors</a>&nbsp;limit&nbsp;the amount of power delivered to each speaker by the power supply.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>A low-pitched, 60-Hertz tone should be heard from the speakers. If the tone sounds too loud, use higher-value resistors.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>With both speakers connected and producing sound, position them so that they are only a foot or two away, facing toward each other. Listen to the volume of the 60 Hz tone.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>Now, reverse the connections (the polarity) of just one of the speakers and note the volume again.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Try switching the polarity of one speaker back and forth from original to reversed, comparing volume levels each way. What do you notice?</p>
<p>By reversing wire connections to one speaker, you are reversing the phase of that speaker&rsquo;s sound wave in reference to the other speaker. In one mode, the sound waves will reinforce one another for a strong volume. In the other mode, the sound waves will destructively interfere, resulting in diminished volume. This phenomenon is common to all wave events: sound waves, electrical signals (voltage waves), waves in water, and even light waves!</p>
<p>Multiple speakers in a stereo sound system must be properly phased&nbsp;so that their respective sound waves don&rsquo;t cancel each other, leaving less total sound level for the listeners&nbsp;to hear. So, even in an AC system where there really is no such thing as constant polarity,&nbsp;the sequence of wire connections may make a significant difference in system performance.</p>
<p>This principle of volume reduction by destructive interference may be exploited for noise cancellation.&nbsp;Such systems sample the waveform of the ambient noise, then produce an identical sound signal 180<sup>o</sup> out of phase with the noise. When the two sound signals meet, they cancel each other out, ideally eliminating all the noise.</p>
<p>As one might guess, this is much easier accomplished with noise sources of steady frequency and amplitude than in this experiment. Cancellation of random, broad-spectrum noise is difficult, as some sort of signal-processing circuit must sample the noise and generate precisely the right amount of cancellation sound at just the right time in order to be effective.</p>
