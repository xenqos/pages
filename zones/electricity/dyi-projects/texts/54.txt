# Si Lab - Zener Diode Voltage Regulator
## Project Overview
<p><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/zener-diodes/" target="_blank">Zener diodes</a> are frequently used as <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-9/voltage-regulation/" target="_blank">voltage regulating</a> devices because they act to clamp the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drop</a> across themselves at a predetermined level. In this project, you will build and test a simple Zener diode voltage regulator, as illustrated in Figure 1, for a number of different supply <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltages</a>.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/zener_diode_voltage_regulator_figure_1.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1.&nbsp;</strong>Test setup for measuring the voltage of a Zener diode voltage regulator.</em>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>Four 6 V batteries</li>
<li>Zener diode, 12 V&mdash;type 1N4742</li>
<li>One 10 k&Omega; <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistor</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Any low-voltage <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/zener-diodes/" target="_blank">Zener diode</a> is appropriate for this experiment. The 1N4742 model listed here (Zener voltage = 12 V) is but one suggestion.&nbsp;Whatever <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/introduction-to-diodes-and-rectifiers/" target="_blank">diode</a> model you choose, I highly recommend one with a Zener voltage rating greater than the voltage of a single battery for a maximum learning experience. It is important that you see how a Zener diode functions when exposed to a voltage less than its breakdown rating.</p>

## Learning Objectives
<ul>
<li>Understand the function of Zener diodes</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Build the simple circuit shown in the circuit schematic of Figure 2 and illustrated in Figure 3.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Be&nbsp;sure to connect the Zener diode in a reverse-bias&nbsp;orientation&nbsp;(cathode positive and anode negative).</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:</strong>&nbsp;Measure the voltage&nbsp;across the diode with one battery as a power source. Record this voltage drop for future reference.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:</strong>&nbsp;Measure and record the voltage drop across the 10 k&Omega; resistors.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>Modify the circuit by connecting two 6 V batteries <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-series-circuits/" target="_blank">in series</a> for a 12 V total power source voltage.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Re-measure the diode&rsquo;s voltage drop, as well as the resistor&rsquo;s voltage drop, with a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-2/voltage-usage/" target="_blank">voltmeter</a>, as illustrated above in Figure 1.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6:&nbsp;</strong>Connect three, then four, and 6 V batteries together in series, forming an 18 V and 24 V power source, respectively.&nbsp;Measure and record the diode&nbsp;and resistor&rsquo;s voltage drops for each new power supply voltage.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Questions to consider:</p>
<ul>
<li>What do you notice about the diode&rsquo;s voltage drop for these four different source voltages?</li>
<li>Do you see how the diode voltage never exceeds a level of 12 V?</li>
<li>What do you notice about the resistor&rsquo;s voltage drop for these four different source voltage levels?</li>
</ul>
<p>The reverse-biased Zener diode clamps the voltage drop at their breakdown voltage. Above this Zener voltage, the Zener diode conducts a lot more current so that any&nbsp;excess voltage supplied by the power source is dropped across the 10 k&Omega; series resistor.</p>
<p>However, it is important to note that a Zener diode cannot make up for a deficiency in source voltage (unlike some types of voltage regulators). For instance, this 12 V Zener diode does not drop 12 V when the power source is only 6 V. It is helpful to think of a Zener diode as a voltage limiter that establishes a maximum voltage drop but not a minimum voltage drop.</p>
