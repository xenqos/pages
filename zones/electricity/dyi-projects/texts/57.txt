<h1>Si Lab - Light Sensor (LED Photodetector)</h1>
<h3>Project Overview</h3>
<p>We typically use <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/special-purpose-diodes/" target="_blank">light-emitted diodes (LEDs)</a> to generate light. However, in this project, we will see LEDs can also be used as crude light sensors. The LED is connected to the base of a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-4/bipolar-junction-transistors-bjt/" target="_blank">bipolar junction transistor (BJT)</a>, as illustrated in Figure 1.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/pulsed-light-sensor-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Schematic diagram of an LED photodetector with amplifier and audio output.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The emitter of the BJT is <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/an-introduction-to-ground/" target="_blank">grounded</a> in&nbsp;a common emitter configuration. Any fluctuations in the base <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">current</a> will be amplified by the BJT as collector current. The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-15/magnetic-fields-and-inductance/" target="_blank">inductance</a> of the <a href="https://eepower.com/power-electronics-textbook/vol-i-electrical-power-systems-design/chapter-5-impedance-matching-and-power-transfer/understanding-electrical-transformers/" target="_blank">transformer</a> then converts these changes in current to a voltage that is passed to the headphones.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<h3>Instructions</h3>

Connect&nbsp;the headphone stereo plug to the transformer&rsquo;s secondary (6 V) winding, as illustrated in Figure 2.&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Place the circuit near a strong, variable light source and listen through the headphones. Sources of light suitable for this experiment include fluorescent and neon lamps, which blink rapidly with the 60 Hz AC power energizing them. You may also try using bright sunlight for a steady light source, then waving your fingers in front of the LED.</p>
<p>This circuit detects pulses of light striking the LED and converts them into relatively strong audio signals to be heard through the headphones. LEDs have the ability to produce current when exposed to light, in a manner not unlike a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-11/special-purpose-batteries/" target="_blank">semiconductor solar cell</a>.&nbsp;By itself, the LED does not produce enough electrical power to drive the audio detector circuit, so a transistor is used to amplify the LED&rsquo;s signals. If the LED is exposed to a pulsing source of light, a tone will be heard in the headphones.</p>
<p>The rapidly passing shadows will cause the LED to generate pulses of voltage, creating a brief buzzing&nbsp;sound in the headphones. LEDs serving as <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/special-purpose-diodes/" target="_blank">photo-detectors</a> are narrow-band devices, responding to a narrow band of wavelengths close, but not identical, to that normally emitted.&nbsp;Infrared remote controls are a good illumination source for near-infrared LEDs employed as photo-sensors, producing a receiver sound.</p>
<p>With a little imagination, it is not difficult to grasp the concept of transmitting audio information&mdash;such as music or speech&mdash;over a beam of pulsing light. Given a suitable transmitter&nbsp;circuit to pulse an LED on and off with the positive and negative crests of an audio waveform from a microphone, the receiver&nbsp;circuit shown here would convert those light pulses back into audio signals.</p>
