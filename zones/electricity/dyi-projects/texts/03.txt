<h3>Introduction to Measuring Voltage</h3>
<p>In all of the experiments in this book, you will be using some test equipment to measure aspects of electricity you cannot directly see, feel, hear, taste, or smell.&nbsp;Electricity&mdash;at least in small, safe quantities&mdash;is insensible by our human bodies.</p>
<p>Your most fundamental &ldquo;eyes&rdquo; in the world of electricity and electronics will be a device called a <em>multimeter</em>.&nbsp;Multimeters indicate the presence of and measure the quantity of electrical properties such as <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/voltage-current-resistance-relate/" target="_blank">voltage, current, and resistance</a>.&nbsp;In this experiment, you will familiarize yourself with the measurement of voltage.</p>
<p>Voltage is the measure of electrical &ldquo;push&rdquo; ready to motivate charges to move through a conductor.&nbsp;In scientific terms, it is the specific energy per unit charge, mathematically defined as joules per coulomb.&nbsp;It is analogous to pressure in a fluid system: the force that moves fluid through a pipe and is measured in the unit of the Volt (V).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Digital Versus Analog Multimeters</h3>
<p>As illustrated in Figure 3, there are two types of multimeters: digital and analog.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/multimeter-digital-analog.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 3. </strong>Digital and analog multimeters for measuring voltage, current, and resistance</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Digital multimeters have numerical displays, like digital clocks, for indicating the quantity of voltage, current, or resistance.&nbsp;Analog multimeters indicate these quantities by means of a moving pointer over a printed scale.&nbsp;Your multimeter should come with some basic instructions.&nbsp;Read them well!</p>
<p>If your multimeter is digital, it will require a small battery to operate.&nbsp;If it is analog, it does not need a battery to measure voltage.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Manual&nbsp;Versus Autoranging Multimeters</h3>
<p>Manual-ranging meters have several different selector positions for each basic quantity: several for voltage, several for current, and several for resistance. For voltage, these ranges could be &mu;V, mV, and V.</p>
<p>Some digital multimeters are autoranging. An autoranging meter has only a few selector switch (dial) positions.&nbsp;Autoranging is&nbsp;to manual ranging as an automatic transmission is to a manual transmission in a car.&nbsp;Basically, an autoranging meter &ldquo;shifts gears&rdquo; automatically to find the best measurement range to display the particular quantity being measured.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Instructions</h3>
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Insert your test probes into the voltmeter. By convention, black probes are used with the negative, or common, input&nbsp;of the voltmeter, while red probes are used with the positive input (see Figure 3).&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>Set your multimeter&rsquo;s selector switch to the highest-value DC volt&nbsp;position available.&nbsp;Autoranging multimeters may only have a single position for DC voltage, in which case you need to set the switch to that one position.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Touch the red test probe to the positive (+) side of a battery and the black test probe to the negative (-) side of the same battery.&nbsp;The voltmeter should now provide you with an indication of the battery&#39;s voltage.&nbsp;If your meter is a manual-range type, and the selector switch has been set to a high-range position, the indication will be small.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>Reverse the test probe connections to the battery if the meter&rsquo;s indication is negative (on an analog meter, a negative value is indicated by the pointer deflecting left instead of right).</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>If you have a manual-ranging voltmeter, move the selector switch to the next lower DC voltage range setting and reconnect to the battery.&nbsp;The indication should be stronger now, as indicated by a greater deflection of the analog meter pointer (needle) or more active digits on the digital meter display.</p>
<p>For best results, move the selector switch to the lowest-range setting that does not over-range the meter.&nbsp;An over-ranged analog meter is said to be &ldquo;pegged,&rdquo; as the needle will be forced all the way to the right-hand side of the scale, past the full-range scale value.&nbsp;Additionally, an over-ranged digital meter sometimes displays the letters &ldquo;OL&rdquo; or a series of dashed lines. This indication is manufacturer-specific.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6: </strong>What happens if you only touch one of the voltmeter test probes to one end of a battery?&nbsp;How does the meter have to connect to the battery to provide an indication?&nbsp;What does this tell us about voltmeter use and the nature of voltage?&nbsp;Is there such a thing as voltage &ldquo;at&rdquo; a single point?</p>
<p><strong>Step 7:&nbsp;</strong>Be sure to measure more than one size of the battery, and learn how to select the best voltage range on the multimeter to give you the maximum indication without over-ranging.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Measuring the Voltage Generated by Light on an LED</h4>
<p><strong>Step 8:&nbsp;</strong>Now switch your multimeter to the lowest DC voltage range available, and touch the meter&rsquo;s test probes to the terminals (wire leads) of the light-emitting diode (LED) as illustrated in Figure 4.&nbsp;&nbsp;A LED is designed to produce light when powered by a small amount of electricity, but LEDs also happen to generate DC voltage when exposed to light, somewhat like a solar cell.&nbsp;Point the LED towards a bright source of light with your multimeter connected to it, and note the meter&rsquo;s indication:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/light-emitting-diode.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 4.&nbsp;</strong>Measuring the voltage induced in a LED when exposed to light.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Batteries develop electrical voltage through chemical reactions. When a battery dies,&nbsp;it has exhausted its original store of chemical fuel.&nbsp;The LED, however, does not rely on an internal fuel&nbsp;to generate voltage; rather, it converts optical energy into electrical energy.&nbsp;So long as there is light to illuminate the LED, it will produce voltage.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Measuring the Voltage of a Motor Functioning as an Electric Generator&nbsp;</h4>
<p><strong>Step 9:&nbsp;</strong>Another source of voltage through energy conversion is an electric&nbsp;generator.&nbsp;Connect your voltmeter (your multimeter, set to the volt&nbsp;function) to the motor&rsquo;s terminals just as you connected it to the LED&rsquo;s terminals, as illustrated in Figure 5.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/jumper-wires-with-alligator-clip.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 5. </strong>Measuring the voltage created by a motor functioning as an electric generator.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Step 10:</strong>&nbsp;Spin the shaft with your fingers.&nbsp;The meter should indicate voltage by means of needle deflection (analog) or numerical readout (digital).&nbsp;The small electric motor specified in the &ldquo;Parts and Materials&rdquo; list functions as an electrical generator if its shaft is turned by a mechanical force.</p>
<p>It may be&nbsp;difficult to maintain both meter test probes in connection with the motor&rsquo;s terminals while simultaneously spinning the shaft with your fingers. That is why Figure 3 uses alligator clip jumper&nbsp;wires.</p>
<p><strong>Step 11: </strong>Next, determine the relationship between voltage and generator shaft speed.</p>
<p><strong>Step 12:&nbsp;</strong>Reverse the generator&rsquo;s direction of rotation and note the change in meter indication.&nbsp;When you reverse shaft rotation, you change the polarity of the voltage created by the generator.&nbsp;The voltmeter indicates polarity by direction of needle direction (analog) or sign of numerical indication (digital).</p>
<p>When the red test lead is positive (+) and the black test lead negative (-), the meter will register voltage in the normal direction.&nbsp;If the test probes are connected with reverse polarity (negative on red and positive on black), the meter will indicate &ldquo;backwards.&rdquo;</p>
