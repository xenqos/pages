<h1>555 Lab - Blue LED Flasher With Voltage Doubler</h1>
<h3>Project Overview</h3>
<p>The circuit of Figure 1 used in this project adds a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/voltage-multipliers/" target="_blank">voltage doubler</a> feature to the&nbsp;circuit and concepts from these two experiments:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-8/cmos-555-long-duration-minimum-parts-red-led-flasher/" target="_blank">555 Lab - Red LED Flasher</a></li>
<li><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-8/cmos-555-long-duration-red-led-flasher/" target="_blank">555 Lab - Advanced Red LED Flasher</a></li>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/practical-application-for-RC-time-constant-circuit.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1.</strong> Schematic diagram of the 555 timer LED flasher with high voltage LED drive.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Blue and white <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/special-purpose-diodes/" target="_blank">light-emitting diodes (LEDs)</a> have a higher Vf (<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/introduction-to-diodes-and-rectifiers/" target="_blank">forward dropping voltage</a>) than other colors, typically around 3.6 V. Therefore, 3 V batteries can&rsquo;t drive them without help. Thus, the need for extra circuitry will be added to this experiment.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The <a href="https://eepower.com/capacitor-guide/" target="_blank">capacitor</a>, C3, is used to double the voltage of this pulse across the blue LED, but it can only do this for a short time. Measuring the current through the LED is impractical with this circuit because of the short pulse duration.</p>

<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Build the 555 oscillator circuit shown in Figures 1 and 2.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/555-lab-blue-led-flasher-breadboard.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Breadboard implementation of the 555 timer LED flasher with high voltage LED drive.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The LED should flash approximately every 1 second.&nbsp;As in the previous circuits, the LED is given a 0.03-second (30 ms) pulse.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2 (optional):&nbsp;</strong>This particular design can also be used with a single 1.5 V battery.&nbsp;If you want to try a 1.5 V battery, change R5 to 10 &Omega; and use a red LED with a better CR1 diode.&nbsp;CR1 is not the best choice for this component, it was selected because it is a common part and it works. Almost any diode will work in this application.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/fast-ultrafast-soft-standard-schottky-selecting-the-right-rectifier/" target="_blank">Schottky</a> and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/characteristics-of-different-photodiode-technologies/" target="_blank">germanium diodes</a> drop much less voltage than silicon diodes. A&nbsp;silicon diode drops 0.6-0.7 V, while a Schottky diode drops 0.1-0.2 V, and a germanium diode drops 0.2 V-0.3 V. If Schottky or germanium diodes&nbsp;are used,&nbsp;the reduced <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drop</a> would translate into a higher voltage across the LED and&nbsp;increased intensity, as the circuit&#39;s efficiency is increased.</p>

<h3>Voltage Doubler Theory of Operation</h3>
<p>In this circuit, Q2 is used as a switch. When Q2 is off, C3 is charged to the battery voltage minus the diode voltage drop of CR1, as shown in Figure 3.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/practical-application-for-RC-time-constant-circuit-1_redo.jpeg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 3.</strong> When transistor switch Q2 is open, capacitor C3 charges.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Since the forward voltage (Vf)&nbsp;of a blue LED&nbsp;is 3.4&nbsp;to 3.6 V, it is not conducting and is effectively out of the circuit.</p>
<p>Figure 4&nbsp;illustrates&nbsp;what happens when Q2 turns on.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/practical-application-for-RC-time-constant-circuit-2_redo.jpeg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 4.</strong> When transistor switch Q2 is closed, the voltage across the diode doubles, turning it on</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The capacitor&nbsp;C3&#39;s positive (+) side is grounded, which moves the negative (-) side to -2.4 V. The diode CR1 is now reverse-biased and is effectively out of the circuit. The -2.4 V is discharged through R5 and D1 to the +3.0 V of the batteries. The total of 5.4 V provides enough voltage to light the blue LED. The 5.4 V value is not quite double the supply voltage because of the loss of diode CR1.&nbsp;Long before C3 is discharged, the circuit switches back, and C3 starts charging again.</p>
<p>You may notice a dim blue glow in the blue LED even when it is off. This demonstrates the difference between theory and practice, 3 V is enough to cause some leakage through the blue LED, even though it is not fully conducting. If you were to measure this current, it would be very small.</p>
