<h1>AC Lab - LC Tank Circuit</h1>
<h3>Project Overview</h3>
<p>If an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-15/practical-considerations-inductors/" target="_blank">inductor</a> and a <a href="https://eepower.com/capacitor-guide/" target="_blank">capacitor</a> are <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">connected in parallel</a> with each other, as illustrated in Figure 1, and then briefly energized by connection to a DC voltage source, oscillations will ensue as energy is exchanged from the capacitor to the inductor and vice versa.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/tank-circuit-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>LC tank circuit schematic diagram.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>These oscillations may be viewed with an oscilloscope connected in parallel with the inductor/capacitor circuit. Parallel inductor and capacitor circuits (LC circuits) are commonly known as <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-6/parallel-tank-circuit-resonance/" target="_blank">tank circuits</a>.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Parts and Materials</h3>
<ul>
<li>Oscilloscope</li>
<li>Assortment of non-polarized capacitors (0.1 &micro;F to 10 &micro;F)</li>
<li><a href="https://eepower.com/power-electronics-textbook/vol-i-electrical-power-systems-design/chapter-5-impedance-matching-and-power-transfer/understanding-electrical-transformers/" target="_blank">Step-down power transformer</a> (120V / 6 V)</li>
<li>10 k&Omega; <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistors</a></li>
<li>6 V battery</li>
</ul>
<p>In this experiment,&nbsp;the power transformer is used simply as an inductor, with only one winding connected. The unused winding should be left open. A simple iron core, single-winding inductor (sometimes known as a <em>choke</em>) may also be used;&nbsp;however, such inductors are more difficult to obtain than power transformers.</p>
<p><strong>Important note:</strong> I recommend NOT using a PC/sound card as an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/worksheets/basic-oscilloscope-operation/" target="_blank">oscilloscope</a> for this experiment because high voltages can be generated by the inductor when the battery is disconnected (<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/inductor-commutating-circuits/" target="_blank">inductive kickback</a>). These high voltages will surely damage the sound card&rsquo;s input and perhaps other portions of the computer as well.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Learning Objectives</h3>
<ul>
<li>To learn how to build a resonant circuit</li>
<li>To determine the effects of capacitor size on the resonant frequency</li>
<li>To learn how to produce antiresonance</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Instructions</h3>
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Build the parallel inductor-capacitor (LC) circuit illustrated by the schematic diagram of Figure 1 and the terminal strip implementation of Figure 2.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/tank-circuit-illustration.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Terminal strip implementation of a resonant&nbsp;LC tank circuit.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>Connect the oscilloscope probe&nbsp;to the high side of the LC circuit.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Touch the battery supply to the parallel LC circuit to energize the circuit. Observe the output on the oscilloscope.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>A tank circuit&rsquo;s natural frequency, called the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/tools/tank-circuit-resonance-calculator/" target="_blank"><em>resonant frequency</em></a>, is determined by the size of the inductor and the size of the capacitor according to the following equation:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$f_{resonance} = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{LC}}$$</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Many small power transformers have primary (120 V) winding inductances of approximately 1 H. Use this value as a rough estimate of inductance for your circuit to calculate the expected oscillation frequency. Compare your calculated frequency with what you observe on the oscilloscope.</p>
<p>Ideally, the oscillations produced by a tank circuit continue indefinitely. However, realistically, oscillations will decay in amplitude over the course of several cycles due to the resistive and magnetic losses of the inductor. Inductors with a high-quality factor, or Q&nbsp;rating, will&nbsp;produce longer-lasting oscillations than low-Q inductors.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Try changing capacitor values and noting the effect on the oscillation frequency. You might notice changes in the duration of oscillations as well due to capacitor size.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6:&nbsp;</strong>Since you know how to calculate the resonant frequency from <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-15/factors-affecting-inductance/" target="_blank">inductance</a> and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-13/factors-affecting-capacitance/" target="_blank">capacitance</a>, can you figure out a way to calculate inductor inductance from known values of circuit capacitance (as measured by a capacitance meter) and resonant frequency (as measured by an oscilloscope)?</p>
<p><strong>Step 7:&nbsp;</strong>Resistance may be intentionally added to the circuit&mdash;either in <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/what-are-series-and-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">series or parallel</a>&mdash;for the express purpose of dampening oscillations. This effect of resistance-dampening tank circuit oscillation is known as an antiresonance. It is analogous to the action of a shock absorber in dampening the bouncing of a car after striking a bump in the road.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>SPICE Simulation of an LC Tank Circuit</h3>
<p>We can simulate the resonance of the LC tank circuit using the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/reference/chpt-7/introduction-to-spice/" target="_blank">SPICE</a>&nbsp;schematic and node numbers shown in Figure 3.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/schematic-with-spice-node-numbers-tank-circuit.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 3. </strong>LC tank circuit schematic diagram for SPICE simulation</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The resistance,&nbsp;R<sub>stray</sub>, is placed in the circuit to dampen oscillations and produce a more realistic simulation. A lower R<sub>stray</sub> value causes longer-lived oscillations because less energy is dissipated. Eliminating this resistor from the circuit results in endless oscillation.</p>
