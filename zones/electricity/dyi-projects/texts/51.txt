<h1>Si Lab - Full-wave Bridge Rectifier</h1>
<h3>Project Overview</h3>
<p>The circuit of Figure 1 uses four <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/introduction-to-diodes-and-rectifiers/" target="_blank">diodes</a> to&nbsp;provide&nbsp;full-wave rectification without the necessity of a center-tapped <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-9/practical-considerations-transformers/" target="_blank">transformer</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/full-wave-bridge-rectifier-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Schematic diagram of the&nbsp;full-wave bridge rectifier circuit driving a motor</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Can you trace the current path from the AC power supply through one diode, through the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-13/introduction-ac-motors/" target="_blank">motor</a>, through a second diode, and back to the AC power supply for both the positive and negative half-cycles of the AC supply voltage?</p>
<p>In applications where a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-5/full-wave-center-tap-rectifier/" target="_blank">center-tapped</a> or <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-10/single-phase-power-systems/" target="_blank">split-phase</a>&nbsp;source is unavailable, this is the only practical method of full-wave rectification. The advantages of the circuit include its simple design and low-cost components.</p>
<p>However, in addition to requiring more diodes than the center-tap circuit, the full-wave bridge suffers a slight performance disadvantage as well.&nbsp;The additional <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drop</a>&nbsp;is caused by the current having to go through two <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/diode-characteristics-circuits/" target="_blank">diodes</a> in each half-cycle rather than through only one.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Parts and Materials</h3>
<ul>
<li>Low-voltage AC power supply (6 V output)</li>
<li>Four <a href="https://www.vishay.com/docs/88503/1n4001.pdf" target="_blank">1N4001</a> rectifying diodes</li>
<li>Small hobby&nbsp;motor, permanent-magnet type</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Learning Objectives</h3>

</ul>

<h5><em><strong>Figure 3.&nbsp;</strong>SPICE circuit schematic for a full-wave bridge rectifier circuit</em></h5>

<p>Netlist (make a text file containing the following text, verbatim):</p>
<pre>
Fullwave bridge rectifier &#10;v1 1 0 sin(0 8.485 60 0 0) &#10;rload 2 3 10k &#10;d1 3 1 mod1 &#10;d2 1 2 mod1 &#10;d3 3 0 mod1 &#10;d4 0 2 mod1 &#10;.model mod1 d &#10;.tran .5m 25m &#10;.plot tran v(1,0) v(2,3) &#10;.end </pre>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This simulation plots the input voltage as a sine wave that oscillates both positively and negatively. The output voltage is a series of positive humps&nbsp;corresponding to both the positive and negative half-cycles of the AC source voltage.&nbsp;The AC source is set to an 8.485 V peak corresponding to a 6 V RMS sine-wave voltage.&nbsp;</p>
