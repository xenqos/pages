# DC Lab - Potato Battery
## Project Overview
<p>In this project, you will build a&nbsp;battery using a potato, a zinc electrode, and a copper electrode. You will measure the&nbsp;voltage&nbsp;with&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-2/voltage-usage/" target="_blank">voltmeter</a> and investigate how the voltage is related to the electrode spacing and surface area (Figure 1).&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/potato-battery-illustration.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1.&nbsp;</strong>Constructing a potato battery and measuring the voltage.</em>

<p>How does a potato battery work?&nbsp;The zinc (on the surface of the galvanized nail or screw) and copper (in the penny or wire) react together to produce&nbsp;chemical energy. The potato acts as an electrolyte between these two dissimilar metals. This chemical energy is then converted to electric energy as the current flows through the circuit.</p>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>One large potato</li>
<li>A strip of zinc, or galvanized metal nail or screw</li>
<li>A piece of thick copper wire and/or a copper penny</li>
</ul>
<p>This&nbsp;basic experiment is based on the use of a potato, however, many fruits (lemons and limes, for example) and&nbsp;vegetables can also work as potential batteries! For the zinc electrode, a large galvanized nail or screw works well. Nails with a thick, rough zinc texture are preferable to galvanized nails that are smooth.</p>

## Learning Objectives
<ul>
<li>To determine the importance of chemical activity in battery operation</li>
<li>To determine how electrode surface area affects battery operation</li>
</ul>

## Instructions
<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Push both the nail and the wire deep into the potato, as illustrated above in Figure 1. Measure the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current-practical-circuit/" target="_blank">voltage</a> output by the potato battery with a voltmeter. Now, wasn&rsquo;t that easy?</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:</strong>&nbsp;Seriously, though, experiment with different metals, electrode depths, and electrode spacings to obtain the greatest voltage possible from the potato. Try increasing the copper surface area by wrapping the wire around a copper penny.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Try other vegetables or fruits and compare voltage output with the same electrode metals.</p>
<p>It can be difficult to power a load with a single &ldquo;potato&rdquo; battery, so don&rsquo;t expect to light up an incandescent lamp, power a hobby motor, or do anything like that. Even if the voltage output is adequate, a potato battery has a fairly high internal <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/resistance/" target="_blank">resistance</a> which causes its voltage to &ldquo;sag&rdquo; badly under even a light load. With multiple potato batteries <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/what-are-series-and-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">connected in series, parallel,</a> or <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-7/what-is-a-series-parallel-circuit/" target="_blank">series-parallel</a> arrangement, though, it is possible to obtain enough <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltage and current</a> capacity to power a small load.</p>
