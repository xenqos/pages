<h4>Measure the Battery Voltage Under Load</h4>
<p><strong>Step 3: </strong>Measure the battery voltage with a voltmeter and record this as well, as it may differ slightly from a no-load condition. We saw this effect exaggerated in the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-3/parallel-batteries/" target="_blank">parallel battery experiment</a> while powering a high-wattage lamp. The battery voltage tends to &ldquo;sag&rdquo; or &ldquo;droop&rdquo; under load.</p>
<p>Although this three-resistor circuit should not present a heavy enough load (not enough current drawn) to cause significant voltage &ldquo;sag,&rdquo; measuring the battery voltage under load is a good scientific practice because it provides more realistic data.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Calculate and Measure the Current</h4>
<p><strong>Step 4:</strong> Use <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-series-circuits/" target="_blank">Ohm&rsquo;s law applied to series circuits</a> to calculate the circuit current.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$I = \frac{V_{battery}}{R_1 + R_2 + R_3}$$</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Step 5: </strong>Then verify this calculated value by measuring the current with an ammeter, as shown in Figure 6, for the terminal strip version of the circuit.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/terminal-strip-circuit.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 6.</strong> Using an ammeter to measure current in a voltage divider circuit.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>If your resistor values are indeed between 1 k&Omega; and 100 k&Omega;, and the battery voltage is approximately 6 V, the current should be a very small value in the milliamp (mA) or microamp (&micro;A) range.&nbsp;</p>
<p>When you measure current with a digital meter, the meter may show the appropriate metric prefix symbol (m or &micro;) in some corner of the display. These metric prefixes are easy to overlook when reading the display of a digital meter, so pay close attention!</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Calculate and Measure the Voltage Drops</h4>
<p><strong>Step 6: </strong>Now, take that calculated value for current and multiply it by the resistance of each resistor to predict their <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drops</a> (V = IR).</p>
<p><strong>Step 7:</strong>&nbsp;Switch your multimeter to the voltage mode and measure the voltage dropped across each resistor, verifying the accuracy of your predictions. Again, there should be close agreement between the calculated and measured voltage figures.</p>
<p>Each resistor voltage drop will be some fraction or percentage of the total voltage, thus this circuit&rsquo;s name, a voltage divider. This fractional value is determined by the ratio of the resistance of the particular resistor and the total resistance.</p>
<p>If a resistor drops 50% of the total battery voltage in a voltage divider circuit, that proportion of 50% will remain the same as long as the resistor values are not altered.&nbsp;So, if the total voltage is 6 V, the voltage across that resistor will be 50% of 6 or 3 V. If the total voltage is 20 V, that resistor will drop 50% of 20 V, which is 10 V.</p>
<p>The equation for the voltage divider ratio of our three resistor circuits is given:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$V_n = \left( \frac{R_n}{R_1 + R_2 + R_3} \right) \cdot V_{source}$$</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Where n = 1, 2, or 3 for the voltage drop across an individual resistor.</p>

<p><strong>Step 9:&nbsp;</strong>Using a digital voltmeter (this is important!), measure voltages around the loop formed by the points 0 to 1, 1 to 2, 2 to 3, and 3 to 0. Write on paper each of these voltages, along with its respective sign as indicated by the meter. In other words, if the voltmeter registers a negative voltage, such as -1.325 volts, you should write that figure as a negative number.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Do not reverse the meter probe connections with the circuit to make the number read &ldquo;correctly.&rdquo; The mathematical sign is very significant in this phase of the experiment! Analog voltmeters do not work with negative voltages, which is why you need a digital voltmeter for this part of the lab.</p>

<p>When algebraically added (algebraically means including the signs of the numbers), the sum of these four voltages should equal zero. This is the fundamental principle of KVL: the algebraic sum of all voltage drops in a &ldquo;loop&rdquo; adds&nbsp;to zero.</p>

<h4>Using KVL on Non-standard Loops</h4>
<p>It is important to realize that the loop stepped around does not have to be the same path that the current takes in the circuit or even a legitimate current path at all. The loop where we tally voltage drops can be any collection of points so long as it begins and ends with the same point.</p>
<p>For example, we may measure and add the voltages in the loop 1-2-3-1, and they will form a sum of zero as well as shown in Figure 14.</p>

<p>Try stepping between any set of points, in any order, around your circuit and see for yourself that the algebraic sum always equals zero. KVL holds true no matter what the configuration of the circuit: series, parallel, series-parallel, or even an irreducible network.</p>
