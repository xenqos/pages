# DC Lab - Potentiometer Voltage Divider
## Project Overview
<p>A <a href="http://eepower.com/resistor-guide/resistor-types/potentiometer/" target="_blank">potentiometer</a> is an&nbsp;adjustable, variable <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistor</a> with three terminals. Two end terminals are connected to the ends of a resistive element, while a&nbsp;third central terminal&nbsp;is connected to an adjustable <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/voltage-divider-circuits/" target="_blank">wiper</a> that sets the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/current-divider-circuits/" target="_blank">resistive divider ratio</a>. If a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltage</a> is applied to the two ends, as illustrated in Figure 1, the central terminal output is an adjustable voltage.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/voltmeter-measuring-output-of-potentiometer.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Using a potentiometer as a variable voltage divider.</em>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>Two 6 V batteries or power supply</li>
<li>Carbon pencil lead&nbsp;for a mechanical-style pencil</li>
<li>Potentiometer, single turn, 5 k&Omega; to 50 k&Omega;, linear taper</li>
<li>Potentiometer, multi-turn, 1 k&Omega; to 20 k&Omega;</li>
</ul>
<p>Potentiometers are&nbsp;manufactured in panel-mount and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/building-simple-resistor-circuits/" target="_blank">breadboard-mount</a>&nbsp;versions.&nbsp;Any potentiometer style will suffice for this experiment; however, we highly recommend a linear potentiometer for this experiment (and for most experiments in general). This is because a linear potentiometer exhibits a direct (linear) correlation between shaft position and voltage division ratio.</p>
<p>As a side note, keep in mind that If you salvage a potentiometer from an old radio or another audio device, you will likely get what is called an <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/resistor-types/potentiometer-taper/" target="_blank">audio taper potentiometer</a>.&nbsp;These potentiometers exhibit a logarithmic relationship between the division ratio and shaft position.</p>

<p>Pencils use a rod made of a graphite-clay mixture, not lead (the metal), to make black marks on paper.&nbsp;Graphite, being a mediocre electrical conductor, acts as a resistor connected across the battery by the two alligator-clip jumper wires.</p>

Move the red probe along the length of the rod and notice the voltmeter&rsquo;s indication change.&nbsp;What probe position gives the greatest voltage value?</p>

### Rotary and Linear Potentiometer Construction
<p>Manufactured potentiometers enclose a resistive strip inside a metal or plastic housing and provide a mechanism for moving a wiper&nbsp;across the length of that resistive strip. Figure 8&nbsp;is an illustration of a rotary potentiometer&rsquo;s construction.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/rotary-potentiometers-construction.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 8.</strong>&nbsp;The construction of a rotary potentiometer.</em>

<p>Some rotary potentiometers have a spiral resistive strip and a wiper that moves axially as it rotates to require multiple shaft turns to drive the wiper from one end of the potentiometer&rsquo;s range to the other. Multi-turn potentiometers are used in applications where the precise setting is important.</p>
<p>Linear potentiometers also contain a resistive strip, the only difference being the wiper&rsquo;s direction of travel.&nbsp;Some linear potentiometers use a slide mechanism to move the wiper. Others use a screw mechanism to facilitate multiple-turn operation, as shown in Figure 9.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/linear-potentiometer-construction-1.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 9.</strong>&nbsp;The construction of a&nbsp;screw-type linear potentiometer</em>

<p>It should be noted that not all linear potentiometers have the same pin assignments. On some, the middle pin is the wiper.</p>
<p>Next, for steps 5 through 10&nbsp;of this experiment, you may use any form of potentiometer construction that is convenient.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Measure the battery voltage while powering the potentiometer&nbsp;and record&nbsp;this voltage figure.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6:&nbsp;</strong>Measure the voltage between the wiper and the potentiometer end connected to the negative (-) side of the battery.</p>
<p><strong>Step 7:&nbsp;</strong>Adjust the potentiometer mechanism until the voltmeter registers exactly 1/3 of the total voltage. For a 6 V battery, this will be approximately 2 V.</p>
<p><strong>Step 8:&nbsp;</strong>Now, connect two batteries in a series-aiding configuration to provide approximately 12 V across the potentiometer, as shown in Figure 10.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/voltmeter-measuring-output-of-potentiometer.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 10. </strong>Connecting two batteries in series to double the applied voltage and measure the potentiometer output voltage.</em>

<p><strong>Step 9:&nbsp;</strong>Measure the total battery voltage, and then measure the voltage between the same two points on the potentiometer (wiper and negative side).</p>
<p><strong>Step 10:&nbsp;</strong>Divide the potentiometer&rsquo;s measured output voltage by the measured total voltage. The quotient should be 1/3, the same voltage division ratio set in Step 8.</p>
