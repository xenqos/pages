# Analog Lab - 555 Oscillator (Astable Multivibrator)
## Project Overview
<p>The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-8/555-ic/" target="_blank">555&nbsp;integrated circuit</a> (IC) is a general-purpose timer useful for various functions. In this experiment, we explore its use as an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-5/audio-oscillator/" target="_blank">astable multivibrator</a> or oscillator. Connected to a <a href="https://eepower.com/capacitor-guide/" target="_blank">capacitor</a> and two <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistors</a>, as shown in Figure 1, it will oscillate freely, driving the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/special-purpose-diodes/" target="_blank">light-emitting diodes (LEDs)</a> on and off with a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-7/square-wave-signals/" target="_blank">square-wave output voltage</a>.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/555-audio-oscillator-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Schematic diagram of an audio oscillator using a 555 timer.</em>

<p>This circuit works on the principle of alternately charging and discharging a capacitor.</p>

## Theory of Operation
<p>When the voltage detected at the threshold (THRESH) pin&nbsp;exceeds 2/3 of the power supply voltage (V<sub>cc</sub>), the 555 begins to discharge the capacitor by <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/an-introduction-to-ground/" target="_blank">grounding</a> the discharge (DISCH)&nbsp;pin.&nbsp;It stops discharging the capacitor when the voltage detected by the trigger (TRIG)&nbsp;terminal&nbsp;falls below 1/3 of the power supply voltage. Thus, when both the THRESH and TRIGH&nbsp;terminals are connected to the capacitor&rsquo;s positive terminal, the capacitor voltage will cycle between 1/3 and 2/3 power supply voltage in a sawtooth&nbsp;pattern.</p>
<p>During the charging cycle, the capacitor receives charging current through the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-series-circuits/" target="_blank">series combination</a> of the 1 M&Omega; and 100 k&Omega; resistors. As soon as the DISCH terminal&nbsp;on the 555 timer goes to ground potential (a transistor inside the 555 connected between that terminal and ground turns on), the capacitor&rsquo;s discharging current only has to go through the 100 k&Omega; resistor. The result is an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-16/complex-circuits/" target="_blank">RC time constant</a> that is much longer for charging than for discharging, resulting in a charging time greatly exceeding the discharging time.</p>
<p>The 555&rsquo;s OUT&nbsp;terminal produces a square-wave voltage signal that is high&nbsp;(nearly V<sub>cc</sub>) when the capacitor is charging and low&nbsp;(nearly 0 V) when the capacitor discharges. This alternating high/low voltage signal drives the two LEDs in opposite modes;&nbsp;when one is on, the other will be off.</p>
<p>Since the capacitor&rsquo;s charging and discharging times are unequal, the high and low times of the output&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/op-amp-and-transistor-analog-square-wave-oscillator-design/" target="_blank">square-wave waveform</a> will also be unequal.&nbsp;This can be seen in the relative brightness of the two LEDs;&nbsp;one will be much brighter than the other because it is on for a longer period of time during each cycle.&nbsp;The equality or inequality between the high&nbsp;and low&nbsp;times of a square wave is expressed as that wave&rsquo;s duty cycle.</p>
<p>A square wave with a 50% duty cycle is perfectly symmetrical;&nbsp;its high&nbsp;time is precisely equal to its low&nbsp;time. A square wave that is high&nbsp;10% of the time and low&nbsp;90% of the time is said to have a 10% duty cycle.</p>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>Two 6 V batteries or a 12 V power supply</li>
<li>One capacitor, 0.1 &micro;F, non-polarized</li>
<li>One 555 timer IC&nbsp;</li>
<li>Two LEDs</li>
<li>One 1 M&Omega; resistor</li>
<li>One 100 k&Omega; resistor</li>
<li>Two 510 &Omega; resistors</li>
<li>Audio detector with headphones</li>
<li><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/basic-waveform-analysis-with-an-oscilloscope/" target="_blank">Oscilloscope</a> (recommended, but not necessary)</li>
</ul>
<p>An oscilloscope would be useful in analyzing the waveforms produced by this circuit, but it is not essential. An audio detector is a useful piece of test equipment for this experiment, especially if you don&rsquo;t have an oscilloscope.</p>
