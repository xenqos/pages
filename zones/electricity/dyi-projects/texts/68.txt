<p>This analog&nbsp;comparator circuit uses an open-loop <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-8/introduction-operational-amplifiers/" target="_blank">operational amplifier (op amp)</a>&nbsp;to compare&nbsp;two voltage signals and determine which one is greater. <h3>Analog Comparator Theory of Operation</h3>
<p>As illustrated in the schematic diagram of Figure 1, the two potentiometers are used to adjust the positive and negative input voltages&nbsp;of the op amp.&nbsp;The&nbsp;op amp compares these two input values and outputs either a high or low voltage based on the following relationships:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$V_+ - V_- &gt; 0 \text{ then } V_{out} = \text{ high}$$&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$V_+ - V_- &lt; 0 \text{ then } V_{out} = \text{ low}$$&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The open-loop output voltage of the op amp is given by:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$V_{out} = Gain \cdot (V_+ - V_-)$$</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Open-loop means that there is no feedback from the op amp output to either of its inputs. Op amps have very high open-loop gain, often on the order of 10,000 or more.&nbsp;Therefore, even a small difference in the input voltages will typically result in the op amp output saturating at either its high or low limits near the upper and lower supply voltages, respectively.</p>
<p>The output status of the op amp is indicated visually by the LED. When the op amp output is high, the LED will be forward-biased and&nbsp;turn on. When the op amp output is low, the LED will be reverse-biased and turn off.</p>

<h3>Applications of Analog Comparators</h3>
<p>Comparator circuits are widely used to compare and monitor physical measurements, provided those physical variables can be translated into voltage signals. For instance, if a small generator were attached to an anemometer wheel to produce a voltage proportional to wind speed, as shown in Figure 4.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/voltage-comparator-schematic-diagram-1.jpg" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 4. </strong>Example application of an analog comparator.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>That wind speed signal could be compared with a set-point, high-limit voltage. The output of the&nbsp;op amp could then drive a high wind speed alarm.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
