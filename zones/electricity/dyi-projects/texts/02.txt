# DIY EE Lab - Setting Up a Home Electronics Lab
## Work Area
<p>A work area should consist of a large workbench, desk, or table (preferably wooden) for performing circuit assembly, with household electrical power (120 V <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-1/what-is-alternating-current-ac/" target="_blank">AC</a>) readily accessible to power soldering equipment, power supplies, and any <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/test-measurement/" target="_blank">test equipment</a>.</p>
<p>Inexpensive desks intended for computer use function nicely&nbsp;for this purpose. It should be noted to avoid a metal-surface desk, as the electrical <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/conductors-insulators-electron-flow/" target="_blank">conductivity</a> of a metal surface creates both a shock hazard and the distinct possibility of unintentional &ldquo;short circuits&rdquo; developing from circuit components touching the metal tabletop. Vinyl and plastic bench surfaces must also be avoided for their ability to generate and store large <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/static-electricity/" target="_blank">static-electric charges</a>, which may damage sensitive electronic components. Also, these materials melt easily when exposed to hot soldering irons and molten solder droplets.</p>
<p>If you cannot obtain a wooden-surface workbench, you may turn any form of table or desk into one by laying a piece of plywood on top. If you are reasonably skilled with woodworking tools, you may construct your own desk using plywood and 2x4 boards.</p>
<p>Finally, the work area should be well-lit and comfortable. I have a small radio set up on my own workbench for listening to music or news as I experiment. My own workbench has a power strip&nbsp;receptacle, and switch assembly mounted to the underside, into which I plug all 120 V devices. It is convenient to have a single switch for shutting off <em>all</em> power in case of an accidental short-circuit!</p>

## Tools
<p>A few tools are required for basic electronics work. Luckily, most of these tools are inexpensive and easy to obtain. If you desire to keep the cost as low as possible, you might want to search for them at thrift stores, pawn shops, and online sources of used electronics&nbsp;before buying them new. As you can tell from these photographs, some of my own tools are rather old but function well nonetheless.</p>

### Digital Multimeter
<p>First and foremost in your tool collection is a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-8/multimeters/" target="_blank">multimeter</a>, as shown in Figure 1.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/digital-multimeter-1.jpg" />&nbsp;</p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1.</strong> Digital multimeter for measuring voltage, current, and resistance.</em>

<p>This is an electrical instrument designed to measure <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltage, current</a>, <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/resistance/" target="_blank">resistance</a>, and other variables as well. Multimeters are manufactured in both <em>digital</em> and <em>analog</em> forms. A digital multimeter is preferred for precision work, but analog meters are useful for gaining an intuitive understanding of instrument sensitivity and range. My own digital multimeter is a Fluke model 27, purchased in 1987.</p>
<p>Most analog multimeters sold today are quite inexpensive and not necessarily precision test instruments. I recommend having both digital and analog meter types in your tool collection, spending as little money as possible on the analog multimeter, and investing in a good-quality digital multimeter (I highly recommend the Fluke brand).</p>

### Sensitive Voltage/Audio Detector
<p>A test instrument I have found indispensable in my work at home is a <em>sensitive voltage detector</em>, or <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/sensitive-audio-detector/" target="_blank"><em>sensitive audio detector</em></a>, described in nearly identical experiments in two chapters of this book volume and shown in Figure 2.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/sensitive-voltage-audio-detector.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Sensitive voltage detector with audio output.</em>

<p>It is nothing more than a sensitized set of audio headphones equipped with an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-1/attenuators/" target="_blank">attenuator</a> (volume control) and limiting <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-3/introduction-to-diodes-and-rectifiers/" target="_blank">diodes</a> to limit sound intensity from strong signals. Its purpose is to audibly indicate the presence of low-intensity voltage signals, DC or AC.</p>
<p>In the absence of an oscilloscope, this is a most valuable tool because it allows you to <em>listen</em> to an electronic signal and, thereby, determine something of its nature. Few tools engender an intuitive comprehension of frequency and amplitude like this! I cite its use in many of the experiments shown in this volume, so I strongly encourage you to build your own. Also, second only to a multimeter, this is the most useful piece of test equipment in the collection of the budget electronics experimenter.&nbsp;</p>
<p>As you can see, I built my detector using scrap parts (a household electrical switch/receptacle box for the enclosure and a section of brown lamp cord for the test leads). Even some of the internal components were salvaged from scrap. For example, the step-down transformer and headphone jack were taken from an old radio purchased in non-working condition from a thrift store. The entire thing, including the headphones, purchased second-hand, cost no more than $15 (at the time) to build. Of course, one could take much greater care in choosing construction materials (metal box, shielded test probe cable), but it probably wouldn&rsquo;t improve its performance significantly.</p>
<p>The single most influential component with regard to detector sensitivity is the headphone assembly. Generally speaking, the greater the dB (decibel)&nbsp;rating of the headphones, the better they will function for this purpose. Since the headphones need not be modified for use in the detector circuit, and they can be unplugged from it, you might justify the purchase of more expensive, high-quality headphones by using them as part of a home entertainment (audio/video) system.</p>

### Wire Stripping Pliers
<p>When working with wire, you need a tool to &ldquo;strip&rdquo; the plastic insulation off the ends, so that bare copper metal is exposed. This tool is called a <em>wire stripper. </em>Two different sizes are&nbsp;shown in Figure 4.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/wire-stripping-pliers.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 4.</strong> Wire stripping pliers.</em>

<p>This is a special form of plier with several knife-edged holes in the jaw area sized just right for cutting through the plastic insulation and not the copper for many wire sizes or gauges.</p>

### Jumper Wires&nbsp;&nbsp;
<p>To make quick, temporary connections between some electronic components, you need jumper wires with small &ldquo;alligator-jaw&rdquo; clips at each end. These may be purchased complete (Figure 5)&nbsp;or assembled from clips and wires (Figure 6).&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/jumper-wires.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 5.</strong> Jumper wires with alligator clips&mdash;commercial version.</em>

#### <em><strong>Figure 6.</strong> Jumper wires with alligator clips&mdash;home-made version.</em>

<p>The homemade jumper wires with large, uninsulated (bare metal) alligator clips are okay to use so long as care is taken to avoid any unintentional contact between the bare clips and any other wires or components. For use in crowded breadboard circuits, commercial jumper wires with insulated (rubber-covered) clips&nbsp;are much preferred.</p>

### Soldering Iron and Rosin-core&nbsp;Solder
<p>For projects involving printed circuit board&nbsp;assembly or repair, a small soldering iron and a spool of rosin-core&nbsp;solder are essential tools. As shown in Figure 9, I recommend a 25-watt soldering iron, no larger for printed circuit board work, and the thinnest solder you can find.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/soldering-iron-and-solder-rosin-core.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 9.</strong>&nbsp;Soldering iron and a spool of rosin-core solder.</em>

<p><strong>Note: do NOT use &ldquo;acid-core&rdquo; solder! </strong>Acid-core solder is intended for the soldering of copper tubes (plumbing), where a small amount of acid helps to clean the copper of surface impurities and provide a stronger bond. If used for electrical work, the residual acid will cause wires to corrode. Also, you should avoid solder that contains&nbsp;lead, opting instead for silver-alloy solder. If you do not already wear glasses, a pair of safety glasses is highly recommended while soldering. This can help&nbsp;prevent bits of molten solder from accidentally landing in your eye should a wire release from the joint during the soldering process and fling bits of solder toward you.</p>

### Hand Drill
<p>Drilling may be required for the assembly of large projects. Although power drills work well, I have found that a simple hand-crank drill (Figure 14) does a remarkable job drilling through plastic, wood, and most metals.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/hand-drill.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 14.</strong>&nbsp;Hand-drill</em>

<p>It is certainly safer and quieter than a power drill and costs quite a bit less.&nbsp;As the wear on my drill indicates, it is an often-used tool around my home!</p>
<p>Some experiments will require a source of audio-frequency voltage signals. Normally, this type of signal is generated in an electronics laboratory by a device called a <em>signal generator</em> or <em>function generator</em>. While building such a device is not impossible (nor difficult!), it often requires the use of an oscilloscope to fine-tune, and oscilloscopes are usually outside the budgetary range of the home experimenter. A relatively inexpensive alternative to a commercial signal generator is an <em>electronic keyboard</em> of the musical type.</p>
<p>You need not be a musician to operate one for the purposes of generating an audio signal (press any key on the board!), and they may be obtained quite readily at second-hand stores for substantially less than the new price. The electronic signal generated by the keyboard is conducted to your circuit via a headphone cable plugged into the headphones&nbsp;jack. More details regarding using a &ldquo;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/musical-keyboard-signal-generator/" target="_blank">Musical Keyboard as a Signal Generator</a>&rdquo; may be found in the experiment of that name in Chapter 4 (AC).</p>

<p>The wire used in solderless breadboards must be 22-gauge, solid copper, as seen in Figure 15.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/spool-of-22-gauge-solid-copper-wire.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 15.</strong>&nbsp;Spool of 22-gauge, solid copper wire.</em>

<p>Spools of this wire are available from electronic supply stores and some hardware stores in different insulation colors. Insulation color has no bearing on the wire&rsquo;s performance, but different colors are sometimes useful for &ldquo;color-coding&rdquo; wire functions in a complex circuit.&nbsp;Note how the last 1/4 inch or so of the copper wire protruding from the spool has been stripped&nbsp;of its plastic insulation.</p>

### Wire Wrap Wire and Wrapping Tool
<p>An alternative to solderless breadboard circuit construction is <em>wire wrap</em>, where 30-gauge (very thin!) solid copper wire is tightly wrapped around the terminals of components inserted through the holes of a fiberglass board. No soldering is required, and the connections made are at least as durable as soldered connections, perhaps more. Wire wrapping requires a spool of this thin wire, and a special wrapping tool, the simplest kind resembling a small screwdriver (Figure 16).</p>

<p><strong><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/wire-wrap-wire-and-wrapping-tool.jpg" /></strong></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 16.</strong>&nbsp;Wire-wrap wire and wrapping tool.</em>

### Electrical Extension Cord
<p>Large wire (14 gauge and bigger) may be needed for building circuits that carry significant levels of current. Though electrical wire of practically any gauge may be purchased on spools, I have found a very inexpensive source of stranded (flexible) copper wire available at any hardware store: cheap extension cords (Figure 17).&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/extension-cord-in-package.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 17.</strong>&nbsp;Electrical extension cord.</em>

<p>Typically comprised of three wires colored white, black, and green, extension cords are often sold at prices less than the retail cost of the constituent wire alone. This is especially true if the cord is purchased on sale! Also, an extension cord provides you with a pair of 120 V connectors: male (plug) and female (receptacle), that may be used for projects powered by 120 V.</p>
<p>To extract the wires, carefully cut the outer layer of plastic insulation away using a utility knife. With practice, you may find you can peel away the outer insulation by making a small cut in it at one end of the cable, then grasping the wires with one hand and the insulation with the other and pulling them apart. This is, of course, much preferable to slicing the entire length of the insulation with a knife, both for safety&rsquo;s sake and for the sake of avoiding cuts in the individual wires&rsquo; insulation.</p>
