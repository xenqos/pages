# DC Lab - Series Batteries
## Project Overview
<p>Connecting <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-11/practical-considerations-batteries/" target="_blank">batteries in series</a>, as shown in Figure 1, means connecting them in line with each other so that there is but a single path for electrons to flow through them all.&nbsp;If you connect batteries so that the positive terminal of one battery connects to the negative of the other, you will find that their respective <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltages</a> add up.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/battery-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Connecting batteries in series and measuring with a voltmeter.</em>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>Two 6 V batteries</li>
<li>One 9 V battery&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Note:</strong> Any size batteries will work for this experiment, but using batteries of different voltages will make the results more interesting</p>

## Instructions
<p><strong>Step 1:</strong> Connect the batteries in series by connecting the positive terminal of one battery to the negative terminal of the other, as illustrated in Figure 2. This creates a single path for the electrons to flow through.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/battery-illustration.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Connecting batteries in series and preparing to measure with a voltmeter.</em>

<p><strong>Step 2:</strong> Measure the voltage across each individual battery and then measure the total voltage across both batteries, as illustrated in Figure 3.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/baterry-connecting-components-in-series.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 3.</strong> Measure the voltages of the individual&nbsp;batteries and the total series voltage.</em>

<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Try connecting batteries of different voltages in series (e.g., a 6 V and a 9 V battery). Record the total voltage.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:</strong> Next, reverse the terminal connections of one of the batteries, as shown in Figure 4, and again measure the total voltage. Compare this to the previous result.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/battery-opposing-series.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 4.</strong> Measure the voltages of the individual&nbsp;batteries and the total series voltage.</em>

<p><strong>Step 5:&nbsp;</strong>Take note of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">polarity of the total voltage</a> as indicated by the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-2/voltage-usage/" target="_blank">voltmeter</a> and the test probe orientation. Remember, if the digital indication is a positive number, the red probe is positive, and the black probe is negative. If the indication is a negative number, the polarity is backward (red = negative, black = positive). Analog meters may not read correctly if reverse-connected as the needle may not be able to move in the opposite direction.</p>
<p><strong>Step 6:</strong>&nbsp;Can you predict what the overall voltage polarity will be based on the polarities of the individual batteries and their respective voltage ratings?</p>
