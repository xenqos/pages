# AC Lab - Using a Transformer to Build a 12 VAC Power Supply
## Project Overview
<p>In this first AC project, you will construct the simple circuit illustrated in Figure 1.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/safe-wiring-techniques-for-power-cords-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 1.</strong> AC step-down transformer circuit schematic diagram.</em>

<p>This circuit uses a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-9/step-up-and-step-down-transformers/" target="_blank">step-down transformer</a> to convert 120 VAC wall voltage to a 12 VAC output with a center tap that can then provide 6 VAC outputs. This is an excellent first project for the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/introduction-to-ac-circuits/" target="_blank">AC Lab series</a>, as the completed circuit can be used in other projects.</p>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li><a href="https://eepower.com/technical-articles/power-transformer-basics-the-magnetic-circuit-part/" target="_blank">Power transformer</a>, 120 VAC step-down to 12 VAC, with the center-tapped secondary winding</li>
<li><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/building-simple-resistor-circuits/" target="_blank">Terminal strip</a> with at least three terminals</li>
<li>Household wall-socket power plug and cord</li>
<li>Line cord switch</li>
<li>Box (optional)</li>
<li>Fuse and fuse holder (optional)</li>
</ul>
<p>If you decide to equip your power supply with a fuse, be sure to get a slow-acting or slow-blow fuse. Transformers may draw high surge&nbsp;currents when initially connected to an AC source, and these transient currents will blow a fast-acting fuse.&nbsp;Determine the proper current rating of the fuse by dividing the transformer&rsquo;s VA&nbsp;rating by 120 V;&nbsp;in other words, calculate the full allowable primary winding current and size the fuse accordingly.</p>

## Learning Objectives
<ul>
<li>To determine the transformer voltage step-down behavior</li>
<li>To determine the purpose of tapped windings</li>
<li>To illustrate safe wiring techniques for power cords</li>
</ul>

## Instructions
<p><span><strong>Warning!</strong> </span>This project involves the use of <strong>dangerous voltages</strong>. You must make sure all high-voltage (120 V household power) conductors are safely insulated from accidental contact. No bare wires should be seen anywhere on the primary (120 V)&nbsp;side of the transformer circuit, illustrated in Figures 1 and 2.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/safe-wiring-techniques-for-power-cords-illustration.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> AC step-down transformer circuit implementation.</em>

<p><strong>Step 1:&nbsp;</strong>Build the circuit as shown in Figures 1 and 2.&nbsp;Be sure to solder all wire connections so that they&rsquo;re secure, and use real electrical tape (not duct tape, scotch tape, packing tape, or any other kind!) to insulate your soldered connections.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2 (Optional):&nbsp;</strong>If you wish to enclose the transformer inside of a box, you may use an electrical junction&nbsp;box obtained from a hardware store or electrical supply house. If the enclosure used is metal rather than plastic, a three-prong plug should be used, with the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/technical-articles/an-introduction-to-ground/" target="_blank">ground</a>&nbsp;prong (the longest one on the plug) connected directly to the metal case for maximum safety.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>Before plugging the plug into a wall socket, do a safety check with an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-2/ohmmeter-usage/" target="_blank">ohmmeter</a>. With the line switch in the on&nbsp;position, measure <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/resistance/" target="_blank">resistance</a> between either plug prong and the transformer case. There should be infinite (maximum) resistance.&nbsp;If the meter registers continuity (some resistance value less than infinity), then you have a short&nbsp;between one of the power <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/conductors-insulators-electron-flow/" target="_blank">conductors</a> and the case, which is dangerous!</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:&nbsp;</strong>Next, check the transformer&#39;s primary windings themselves for continuity. With the line switch in the on&nbsp;position, there should be a small amount of resistance between the two plug prongs.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5: </strong>Now check the transformer&#39;s primary windings with the switch&nbsp;turned off.&nbsp;The resistance indication should increase to infinity (open circuit&mdash;no continuity).</p>
<p><strong>Step 6:&nbsp;</strong>Measure the resistance between pairs of wires on the secondary side. These secondary windings should register much lower resistance&nbsp;than the primary. Why is this?</p>
<p><strong>Step 7:&nbsp;</strong>Plug the cord into a wall socket and turn the switch on. You should be able to measure AC voltage at the secondary side of the transformer, between pairs of terminals. Between two of these terminals, you should measure about 12 V.&nbsp;Between either of these two terminals and the third terminal, you should measure half that. This third wire is the center-tap&nbsp;wire of the secondary winding.</p>
<p><strong>Step 8:&nbsp;</strong>It would be advisable to keep this project assembled for use in powering other <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/" target="_blank">experiments</a> shown in this <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/" target="_blank">online textbook</a>. From here on, I will designate this low-voltage AC power supply&nbsp;using the illustration shown in Figure 3.</p>

## SPICE Simulation of the AC Step-down Transformer
<p>You can simulate, via <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/reference/chpt-7/introduction-to-spice/" target="_blank">SPICE</a>,&nbsp;the AC step-down transformer with the circuit illustrated in Figure 4 and the netlist below.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/schematic-with-spice-node-numbers.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 4.</strong>&nbsp;AC step-down transformer circuit with node numbers and extra devices for SPICE simulation.</em>

<p><ins>Netlist (make a text file containing the following text, verbatim):</ins></p>
<pre>
AC Step-down Transformer with Center-tap Secondary&#10;v1 1 0 ac 120 sin &#10;rbogus1 1 2 1e-3 &#10;l1 2 0 10 &#10;l2 5 4 0.025 &#10;l3 4 3 0.025 &#10;k1 l1 l2 0.999 &#10;k2 l2 l3 0.999 &#10;k3 l1 l3 0.999 &#10;rbogus2 3 0 1e12 &#10;rload1 5 4 1k &#10;rload2 4 3 1k &#10;* Sets up AC analysis at 60 Hz: &#10;.ac lin 1 60 60 &#10;* Prints primary voltage between nodes 2 and 0: &#10;.print ac v(2,0) &#10;* Prints (top) secondary voltage between nodes 5 and 4: &#10;.print ac v(5,4) &#10;* Prints (bottom) secondary voltage between nodes 4 and 3: &#10;.print ac v(4,3) &#10;* Prints (total) secondary voltage between nodes 5 and 3: &#10;.print ac v(5,3) &#10;.end </pre>

<p>Two extra <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistors</a> have been added to the circuit to assist with the simulation. R<sub>bogus1</sub>&nbsp;is a small resistance in series with the primary transformer winding, L<sub>1</sub>. R<sub>bogus2</sub>&nbsp;is a high-value resistance to provide a pseudo-ground connection between the primary and secondary sides of the transformer.&nbsp;</p>
