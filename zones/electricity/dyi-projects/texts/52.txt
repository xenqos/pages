# Si Lab - Full-wave Center-tap Rectifier
## Project Overview
<p>The&nbsp;rectifier circuit&nbsp;in Figure 1 is called full-wave because it makes use of both the positive and negative half-cycles of the sinusoidal AC source voltage wave in powering a DC load.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/full-wave-center-tap-rectifier-schematic-diagram.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 1. </strong>Schematic diagram of a&nbsp;full-wave center-tap&nbsp;rectifier circuit driving a DC motor.</em>

<p>As a result, there is less ripple&nbsp;voltage seen at the load than in a half-wave rectifier. It relies on the center-tapped transformer and two diodes to always provide a positive voltage to the load.</p>

## Parts and Materials
<ul>
<li>Low-voltage AC power supply (6 V&nbsp;output) with a center tap</li>
<li>Two 1N4001 rectifying diodes&nbsp;or any of the 1N400X series of rectifying diodes&nbsp;</li>
<li>Small hobby&nbsp;motor, permanent-magnet type</li>
<li>Optional: Audio detector with headphones</li>
<li>Optional: 0.1 &micro;F capacitor</li>
<li>One toggle switch, SPST (single-pole, single-throw)</li>
</ul>
<p>It is essential for this experiment that the low-voltage AC power supply is equipped with a center tap. A transformer with a non-tapped secondary winding simply will not work for this circuit.</p>

## Learning Objectives
<ul>
<li>Design of a full-wave center-tap rectifier circuit</li>
<li>How to measure ripple&nbsp;voltage with a voltmeter</li>
</ul>

## Instructions
<p><strong>Step 1: </strong>First, build the circuit illustrated in Figure 2.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/full-wave_center-tap_rectifier_figure_2.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 2. </strong>Full-wave center-tap&nbsp;rectifier circuit driving a DC motor.</em>

<p>Note that our low-voltage AC power supply contains inside it the center-tapped step-down transformer that was shown in the schematic diagram of Figure 1. This transformer converts the 120 VAC wall voltage down to the 12 VAC (&plusmn; 6 VAC)&nbsp;output by our supply.</p>
<p><strong>Step 2:&nbsp;</strong>Use a voltmeter to measure both the DC and AC voltage delivered to the motor.&nbsp;The RMS (root-mean-square) value of this full-wave rectifier&rsquo;s output is also greater for this circuit than for the half-wave rectifier.</p>
<p>You should notice the advantages of the full-wave rectifier immediately by the greater DC output voltage&nbsp;and lower, undesirable&nbsp;AC voltage fluctuations, as compared to the half-wave rectifier experiment. In addition, this full-wave rectifier design has only a single-diode voltage drop in the conduction path. This is better than the two-diode voltage drops of the full-wave bridge rectifier.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:&nbsp;</strong>An experimental advantage of this circuit is the ease with which it may be converted&nbsp;to a half-wave rectifier.&nbsp;Simply disconnect the short jumper wire connecting the two diodes&rsquo; cathode ends together on the terminal strip (Figure 2).&nbsp;Better yet, for a quick comparison between half and full-wave rectification, you can add a switch in the circuit to open and close this connection at will using the circuit of Figures 3 and 4.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/full-wave-center-tap-rectifier-schematic-diagram-2.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 3. </strong>Schematic diagram with a switch to select between&nbsp;full-wave and half-wave rectifier operation.</em>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/full-wave_center-tapped_rectifier_figure_4.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 4. </strong>Illustration of the circuit with a switch to select between&nbsp;full-wave and half-wave rectifier operation.</em>

<p>With the ability to quickly switch between half- and full-wave rectification, you may easily perform qualitative comparisons between the two different operating modes.</p>
<p><strong>Step 4 (Optional):&nbsp;</strong>You can use the audio signal detector to listen&nbsp;to the ripple voltage present between the motor terminals for half-wave and full-wave rectification modes, noting both the intensity and the quality of the tone.&nbsp;Remember to use a coupling capacitor in series with the detector, as illustrated in Figure 5, so that it only receives the AC ripple&nbsp;voltage and not the DC voltage.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/articles/full-wave-center-tap-rectifier-illustration-3.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 5. </strong>Configuration of the audio detector for evaluating the rectifier outputs.</em>
