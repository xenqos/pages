<h1>Introduction to Electron Tubes</h1>
<p>An often neglected area of study in modern electronics is that of tubes, more precisely known as vacuum tubes or electron tubes. Almost completely overshadowed by <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/" target="_blank">semiconductor</a>, or &ldquo;solid-state&rdquo; components in most modern applications, tube technology once dominated electronic circuit design.</p>
<p>In fact, the historical transition from &ldquo;electric&rdquo; to &ldquo;electronic&rdquo; circuits really began with tubes, for it was with tubes that we entered into a whole new realm of circuit function: a way of controlling the flow of electrons (<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/voltage-current-resistance-relate/" target="_blank">current</a>) in a circuit by means of another electric signal (in the case of most tubes, the controlling signal is a small voltage). The semiconductor counterpart to the tube, of course, is the transistor. Transistors perform much the same function as tubes: controlling the flow of electrons in a circuit by means of another flow of electrons in the case of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-4/bipolar-junction-transistors-bjt/" target="_blank">bipolar transistor</a>, and controlling the flow of electrons by means of a voltage in the case of the field-effect transistor. In either case, a relatively small electric signal controls a relatively large electric current. This is the essence of the word &ldquo;electronic,&rdquo; so as to distinguish it from &ldquo;electric,&rdquo; which has more to do with how electron flow is regulated by Ohm&rsquo;s Law and the physical attributes of wire and components.</p>
<p>Though tubes are now obsolete for all but a few specialized applications, they are still a worthy area of study. If nothing else, it is fascinating to explore &ldquo;the way things used to be done&rdquo; in order to better appreciate modern technology.</p>
