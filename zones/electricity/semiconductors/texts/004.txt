<h1>Amplifier Gain</h1>
<p><a name="Gain"></a></p>
<h3>The Voltage Gain</h3>
<p>Because <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-1/amplifiers/" target="_blank">amplifiers</a> have the ability to increase the magnitude of an input signal, it is useful to be able to rate an amplifier&rsquo;s amplifying ability in terms of an output/input ratio. The technical term for an amplifier&rsquo;s output/input magnitude ratio is <em>gain</em>. As a ratio of equal units (power out / power in, voltage out / voltage in, or current out / current in), gain is naturally a unitless measurement.</p>
<p>Mathematically, gain is symbolized by the capital letter &ldquo;A&rdquo;.</p>
<h3>How to Calculate Voltage Gain</h3>
<p>For example, if an amplifier takes in an AC voltage signal measuring 2 volts RMS and outputs an AC voltage of 30 volts RMS, it has an AC voltage gain of 30 divided by 2, or 15:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/voltage-gain-equation.png" /></p>
<p>Correspondingly, if we know the gain of an amplifier and the magnitude of the input signal, we can calculate the magnitude of the output. For example, if an amplifier with an AC current gain of 3.5 is given an AC input signal of 28 mA RMS, the output will be 3.5 times 28 mA or 98 mA:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/calculate-the-magnitude-of-the-output.png" /></p>
<p>In the last two examples, I specifically identified the gains and signal magnitudes in terms of &ldquo;AC&rdquo;. This was intentional, and illustrates an important concept: electronic amplifiers often respond differently to AC and DC input signals and may amplify them to different extents.</p>
<p>Another way of saying this is that amplifiers often amplify <em>changes</em> or <em>variations</em> in input signal magnitude (AC) at a different ratio than <em>steady </em>input signal magnitudes (DC). The specific reasons for this are too complex to explain at this time, but the fact of the matter is worth mentioning.</p>
<p>If gain calculations are to be carried out, it must first be understood what type of signals and gains are being dealt with, AC or DC.</p>
<h3>Electrical Amplifier Gains: Voltage, Current, and/or Power</h3>
<p>Electrical amplifier gains may be expressed in terms of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/electrical-quantities-a/" target="_blank">voltage, current, and/or power</a> in both AC and DC.</p>

<p>If multiple amplifiers are staged, their respective gains form an overall gain equal to the product (multiplication) of the individual gains.
