<h1>Attenuators</h1>
<p><a name="Attenuator"></a></p>
<h3>What is Attenuators?</h3>
<p>Attenuators are <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-1/active-versus-passive-devices/" target="_blank">passive devices</a>. It is convenient to discuss them along with decibels. Attenuators weaken or <em>attenuate</em> the high level output of a signal generator, for example, to provide a lower level signal for something like the antenna input of a sensitive radio receiver. (figure below) The attenuator could be built into the signal generator, or be a stand-alone device. It could provide a fixed or adjustable amount of attenuation. An attenuator section can also provide isolation between a source and a troublesome load.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/constant-impedance-attenuator.png" /></p>
<p>Constant impedance attenuator is matched to source impedance ZI and load impedance ZO. For radio frequency equipment Z is 50 &Omega;.</p>
<p>In the case of a stand-alone attenuator, it must be placed in series between the signal source and the load by breaking open the signal path as shown in the figure <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-1/attenuators/#03376.png" title="">above</a>. In addition, it must match both the source impedance <strong>Z</strong><strong>I</strong> and the load impedance <strong>Z</strong><strong>O</strong>, while providing a specified amount of attenuation. In this section we will only consider the special, and most common, case where the source and load impedances are equal. Not considered in this section, unequal source and load impedances may be matched by an attenuator section. However, the formulation is more complex.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/t-section-and-Π-section-attenuators.png" /></p>
<p>T section and &Pi; section attenuators are common forms.</p>
<p>Common configurations are the <strong>T</strong> and <strong>&Pi;</strong> networks shown in the figure above. Multiple attenuator sections may be cascaded when even weaker signals are needed as in the figure below.</p>
<h3>Usage of Decibels for Attenuators</h3>
<p>Voltage ratios, as used in the design of attenuators are often expressed in terms of decibels. The voltage ratio must be derived from the attenuation in decibels. Power ratios expressed as decibels are additive. For example, a 10 dB attenuator followed by a 6 dB attenuator provides 16dB of attenuation overall.</p>
<p>10 dB + 6 db = 16 dB</p>
<p>Changing sound levels are perceptible roughly proportional to the logarithm of the power ratio (PI / PO).</p>
<p>sound level = log10(PI / PO)</p>
<p>A change of 1 dB in sound level is barely perceptible to a listener, while 2 db is readily perceptible. An attenuation of 3dB corresponds to cutting power in half, while a gain of 3 db corresponds to a doubling of the power level. A gain of -3 dB is the same as an attenuation of +3 dB, corresponding to half the original power level.</p>
<p>The power change in decibels in terms of power ratio is:</p>
<p>dB = 10 log10(PI / PO)</p>
<p>Assuming that the load RI at PI is the same as the load resistor RO at PO(RI = RO), the decibels may be derived from the voltage ratio (VI / VO) or current ratio (II / IO):</p>
<p>PO = V O IO = VO2 / R = IO2 R PI = V I II = VI2 / R = II2 R dB = 10 log10(PI / PO) = 10 log10(VI2 / VO2) = 20 log10(VI/VO) dB = 10 log10(PI / PO) = 10 log10(II2 / IO2) = 20 log10(II/IO)</p>
<h3>Decibel Equations</h3>
<p>The two most often used forms of the decibel equation are:</p>
<p>dB = 10 log10(PI / PO) or dB = 20 log10(VI / VO)</p>
<p>We will use the latter form, since we need the voltage ratio. Once again, the voltage ratio form of equation is only applicable where the two corresponding resistors are equal. That is, the source and load resistance need to be equal.</p>
<h4>Examples using the Decibel Equations</h4>
<p><strong>Example: </strong>Power into an attenuator is 10 Watts, the power out is 1 Watt. Find the attenuation in dB.</p>
<p>dB = 10 log10(PI / PO) = 10 log10 (10 /1) = 10 log10 (10) = 10 (1) = 10 dB</p>
<p><strong>Example: </strong>Find the voltage attenuation ratio (K= (VI / VO)) for a 10 dB attenuator.</p>
<p>dB = 10= 20 log10(VI / VO) 10/20 = log10(VI / VO) 1010/20 = 10log10(VI / VO) 3.16 = (VI / VO) = AP(ratio)</p>
<p><strong>Example: </strong>Power into an attenuator is 100 milliwatts, the power out is 1 milliwatt. Find the attenuation in dB.</p>
<p>dB = 10 log10(PI / PO) = 10 log10 (100 /1) = 10 log10 (100) = 10 (2) = 20 dB</p>
<p><strong>Example: </strong>Find the voltage attenuation ratio (K= (VI / VO)) for a 20 dB attenuator.</p>
<p>dB = 20= 20 log10(VI / VO ) 1020/20 = 10 log10(VI / VO ) 10 = (VI / VO ) = K</p>
<h3>T-section attenuator</h3>
<p>The T and &Pi; attenuators must be connected to a&nbsp;<strong>Z</strong>&nbsp;source and&nbsp;<strong>Z</strong>&nbsp;load impedance. The&nbsp;<strong>Z</strong>-(arrows) pointing away from the attenuator in the figure below indicate this. The&nbsp;<strong>Z</strong>-(arrows) pointing toward the attenuator indicates that the impedance seen looking into the attenuator with a load Z on the opposite end is Z, Z=50 &Omega; for our case. This impedance is a constant (50 &Omega;) with respect to attenuation&ndash; impedance does not change when attenuation is changed.</p>

<p>Telephone utility and other audio work often requires matching to 600 &Omega;. Multiply all <strong>R</strong> values by the ratio (600/50) to correct for 600 &Omega; matching. Multiplying by 75/50 would convert table values to match a 75 &Omega; source and load.</p>

<p>The amount of attenuation is customarily specified in dB (decibels). Though, we need the voltage (or current) ratio <strong>K</strong> to find the resistor values from equations. See the <strong>dB/20</strong> term in the power of <strong>10</strong> term for computing the voltage ratio <strong>K</strong> from dB, above.</p>
<p>The <strong>T</strong> (and below <strong>&Pi;</strong>) configurations are most commonly used as they provide bidirectional matching. That is, the attenuator input and output may be swapped end for end and still match the source and load impedances while supplying the same attenuation.</p>
<p>Disconnecting the source and looking in to the right at <strong>V</strong><strong>I</strong>, we need to see a series parallel combination of <strong>R</strong><strong>1</strong>, <strong>R</strong><strong>2</strong>, <strong>R</strong><strong>1</strong>, and <strong>Z</strong> looking like an equivalent resistance of <strong>Z</strong><strong>IN</strong>, the same as the source/load impedance Z: (a load of Z is connected to the output.)</p>
<p>ZIN = R1 + (R2 ||(R1 + Z))</p>
<p>For example, substitute the 10 dB values from the 50 &Omega; attenuator table for <strong>R</strong><strong>1</strong> and <strong>R</strong><strong>2</strong> as shown in the figure below.</p>
<p>ZIN = 25.97 + (35.14 ||(25.97 + 50)) ZIN = 25.97 + (35.14 || 75.97 ) ZIN = 25.97 + 24.03 = 50</p>
<p>This shows us that we see 50 &Omega; looking right into the example attenuator (figure below) with a 50 &Omega; load.</p>
<p>Replacing the source generator, disconnecting load <strong>Z</strong> at <strong>V</strong><strong>O</strong>, and looking in to the left, should give us the same equation as above for the impedance at <strong>V</strong><strong>O</strong>, due to symmetry. Moreover, the three resistors must be values which supply the required attenuation from input to output. <h3>Cascaded sections</h3>
<p>Attenuator sections can be cascaded as in the figure&nbsp;below&nbsp;for more attenuation than may be available from a single section. For example two 10 db attenuators may be cascaded to provide 20 dB of attenuation, the dB values being additive. The voltage attenuation ratio&nbsp;<strong>K</strong>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<strong>V</strong><strong>I</strong><strong>/V</strong><strong>O</strong>&nbsp;for a 10 dB attenuator section is 3.16. The voltage attenuation ratio for the two cascaded sections is the product of the two&nbsp;<strong>K</strong>s or 3.16x3.16=10 for the two cascaded sections.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/cascaded-attenuator-sections.png" /></p>
<p>Cascaded attenuator sections: dB attenuation is additive.</p>
<p>Variable attenuation can be provided in discrete steps by a switched attenuator. The example in the figure below, shown in the 0 dB position, is capable of 0 through 7 dB of attenuation by additive switching of none, one or more sections.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/switched-attenuator.png" /></p>
<p>Switched attenuator: attenuation is variable in discrete steps.</p>
<p>The typical multi section attenuator has more sections than the above figure shows. The addition of a 3 or 8 dB section above enables the unit to cover to 10 dB and beyond. Lower signal levels are achieved by the addition of 10 dB and 20 dB sections, or a binary multiple 16 dB section.</p>
<h3>RF attenuators</h3>
<p>For radiofrequency (RF) work (&lt;1000 Mhz), the individual sections must be mounted in shielded compartments to thwart capacitive coupling if lower signal levels are to be achieved at the highest frequencies. The individual sections of the switched attenuators in the previous section are mounted in shielded sections. Additional measures may be taken to extend the frequency range to beyond 1000 Mhz. This involves construction from special shaped lead-less resistive elements.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/coaxial-t-section-attenuator.png" /></p>
<p>A coaxial T-section attenuator consisting of resistive rods and a resistive disk is shown in the figure above. This construction is usable to a few gigahertz. The coaxial &Pi; version would have one resistive rod between two resistive disks in the coaxial line as in the figure below.</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/coaxial-Π-attenuator.png" /></p>
<p>RF connectors, not shown, are attached to the ends of the above T and &Pi; attenuators. The connectors allow individual attenuators to be cascaded, in addition to connecting between a source and load. For example, a 10 dB attenuator may be placed between a troublesome signal source and an expensive spectrum analyzer input. Even though we may not need the attenuation, the expensive test equipment is protected from the source by attenuating any overvoltage.</p>
<p><strong>Summary: Attenuators</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>An <em>attenuator</em> reduces an input signal to a lower level.</li>
<li>The amount of attenuation is specified in <em>decibels</em> (dB). Decibel values are additive for cascaded attenuator sections.</li>
<li>dB from power ratio: dB = 10 log10(PI / PO)</li>
<li>dB from voltage ratio: dB = 20 log10(VI / VO)&nbsp;</li>
<li><em>T</em> and <em>&Pi;</em> section attenuators are the most common circuit configurations.</li>
</ul>
