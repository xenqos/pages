<h1>JFET Biasing Techniques</h1>
<p>In analog design, establishing the right operating point for active components is crucial to achieving the desired performance. In the case of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-5/junction-field-effect-transistors-jfet/" target="_blank">JFETs</a>, appropriate biasing techniques can make the circuit less sensitive to variations in device parameters. In this chapter, we&rsquo;ll explain different biasing methods for JFET devices using an example.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>The JFET Biasing Problem Statement</h3>
<p>Consider an n-channel JFET with I<sub>DSS</sub> = 8 mA and V<sub>P</sub> = -2 V. The transfer characteristic of the JFET in the saturation region is shown in Figure 1.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_1.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 1.</strong> N-channel JFET I-V curve in saturation.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>With that in mind, let&#39;s assume that the goal is to bias the JFET at point Q corresponding to i<sub>D&nbsp;</sub>= 2 mA and v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= -1 V. There are a few basic biasing methods that we&rsquo;ll explore below.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Biasing Method 1&mdash;the Constant Voltage Bias</h3>
<p>The simplest biasing scheme, known as constant voltage bias, is shown in Figure 2.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_2.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 2.</strong> Constant voltage biasing circuit for an n-channel JFET.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Under normal conditions, the gate current of a JFET is essentially zero, and no <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drops</a> across R<sub>G</sub>. Thus, the voltage source, V<sub>GG,</sub> directly appears across the source-gate junction&mdash;i.e., v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= -V<sub>GG</sub>. By choosing V<sub>GG</sub> = 1, we can set the bias point to v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= -1 V. To find the drain current, we can simply substitute the following into Shockley&#39;s equation, shown in Equation 1:</p>
<ul>
<li>I<sub>DSS</sub> = 8 mA</li>
<li>V<sub>P</sub> = -2 V</li>
<li>v<sub>GS</sub> = -1 V</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$i_D=I_{DSS} \big ( 1- \frac{v_{GS}}{V_P} \big )^2$$</p>
<h5><em><strong>Equation 1.</strong></em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Which produces i<sub>D&nbsp;</sub>= 2 mA.</p>
<p>In addition, a graphical approach can be employed to determine the DC operating point of the above circuit. To do this, we find the intersection point of the line v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= -1 with the device characteristic curve (Figure 3).&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_3.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 3. </strong>Determining the JFET current for a specific gate-source voltage.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Advantages of the Graphical Approach</h3>
<p>The graphical approach can help us develop a more intuitive understanding of the circuit operation. As an example, consider the following circuit (Figure 4),&nbsp;where the input AC signal is applied through the coupling <a href="https://eepower.com/capacitor-guide/">capacitor</a>, C<sub>1</sub>.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_4.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 4. </strong>Driving a JFET with an AC-coupled sinusoidal signal.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Let v<sub>in</sub> be a sinusoidal input with amplitude, A, and assume that the coupling capacitor is sufficiently large so the excursions of v<sub>in</sub> appear at the gate terminal with no attenuation. In this case, the AC signal adds algebraically to DC bias voltage. Therefore, v<sub>GS</sub> varies from -1 -A to -1 +A volts. This is illustrated in Figure 5.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_6.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 5. </strong>Effect of sinusoidal gate-source voltage variation on the JFET drain current.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>By finding the intersection point of the lines corresponding to the maximum and minimum of v<sub>GS</sub> with the characteristic curve, we can determine the variation in the drain current. In the above example, i<sub>D</sub> varies from just above 1 mA to slightly higher than 3 mA.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Another advantage of the graphical method is that it helps you visualize how distortion can increase at certain points of the curve. Recall that the slope of the characteristic curve specifies the transconductance of the circuit, which is related to the circuit&rsquo;s gain. Therefore, a larger curvature in the i<sub>D</sub> - v<sub>GS</sub> plot corresponds to a larger distortion. To clarify this, consider the example depicted in Figure 5. Assume that the DC operating point of the circuit is changed to Q<sub>1</sub>, as depicted below in Figure 6.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_5.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 6. </strong>Demonstration of reduced JFET gain as the bias voltage is reduced.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The slope of the curve approaches zero as we get closer and closer to the pinch-off voltage. Therefore, in the above example, the transconductance changes from almost zero to a positive value. Thus, we expect the above circuit to have a larger distortion. Also, note that with the new bias point, the variation in i<sub>D</sub> is much smaller than in the previous case. This means that the new bias point provides a smaller gain.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Biasing Method 2&mdash;the Self-bias Configuration&nbsp;</h3>
<p>To avoid the need for two DC supplies, the self-bias configuration shown in Figure 7 can be used.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_7.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 7. </strong>JFET self-biasing circuit schematic.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this case, the gate is held at zero volts by means of R<sub>G</sub>; however, the source is at a positive potential because the drain current flows through R<sub>S</sub>. As a result, a negative v<sub>GS</sub> is produced without employing a negative voltage supply. Applying <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/kirchhoffs-voltage-law-kvl/" target="_blank">Kirchhoff&rsquo;s voltage law</a> to the input circuit, we get Equation 2.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$v_{GS}=-R_S i_D$$</p>
<h5><em><strong>Equation 2.</strong></em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the case of our example, the bias point is given&mdash;i<sub>D</sub> = 2 mA and v<sub>GS</sub> = -1 V. Substituting these values into the above equation, we obtain R<sub>S</sub> = 0.5 k&Omega;. Usually, R<sub>S</sub> is given, and we need to find the DC operating point. In this case, Equations 2 and 1 should be solved simultaneously to determine the JFET&rsquo;s DC operating point. Alternatively, we can use the graphical approach and find the intersection point of the bias line (Equation 2) with JFET&#39;s characteristic curve. The graphical solution for R<sub>S&nbsp;</sub>= 0.5 k&Omega; is shown in Figure 8.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_8.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 8. </strong>Determining the biasing point for the JFET self-biasing circuit.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Note that the slope of the line is -1/R<sub>S</sub>. Also, observe that the value of R<sub>S</sub> is fixed for a given DC operating point. Now consider applying an AC signal to the self-bias configuration (Figure 9).&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_9.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 9.</strong> AC coupling a signal to the JFET self-biasing circuit.</em></h5>
<h5>&nbsp;</h5>
<p>Again, assume that the sinusoidal input with amplitude, A, is applied to the input,&nbsp;and the whole signal excursions appear at the gate terminal with no attenuation. Let&rsquo;s see how the graphical method can be used to find the amplitude of variations in v<sub>GS</sub> and i<sub>D</sub>. When the input is at its maximum, the gate voltage is raised from 0 to A volts. Therefore, the bias line is modified to:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$A=v_{GS}+R_S i_D$$&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the i<sub>D&nbsp;</sub>- v<sub>GS</sub> plane, the above equation corresponds to a line with a slope of -1/R<sub>S</sub> that intercepts the horizontal axis at v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= A (the orange line in Figure 10).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_10.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 10. </strong>JFET drain current variation as a function of the gate-source voltage variation.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Similarly, when the AC input is at its minimum, the bias line is a line with a slope of -1/R<sub>S</sub> that intercepts the horizontal axis at v<sub>GS&nbsp;</sub>= -A (the pink line in the above figure). The intersection points of these lines with the characteristic curve specify the variation in v<sub>GS</sub> and i<sub>D</sub>.</p>

<h3>Biasing Method 3&mdash;the Voltage Divider Biasing&nbsp;&nbsp;</h3>
<p>A disadvantage of the self-bias circuit is that the slope of the bias line, which is equal to -1/R<sub>S</sub>, is fixed for a given DC operating point (Figure 8). In the following chapters&nbsp;of this textbook, we&rsquo;ll discuss how the ability to choose the slope of the bias line can reduce the sensitivity of the DC operating point to device parameter variations. The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/voltage-divider-circuits/" target="_blank">voltage divider</a> biasing arrangement, depicted in Figure 15, enables us to choose the slope of the bias line arbitrarily.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_15.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 15. </strong>Voltage divider biasing of a JFET.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>To find the bias line, note that the gate current is essentially zero, and thus, the gate voltage is specified by the resistive voltage divider made of R<sub>1</sub> and R<sub>2</sub>. Therefore, the gate voltage can be expressed as Equation 3.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$v_{G}=\frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}V_{DD}$$</p>
<h5><em><strong>Equation 3.</strong></em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, applying Kirchhoff&rsquo;s voltage law to the input circuit produces:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>$$\frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}V_{DD}=v_{GS}+R_S i_D$$</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the i<sub>D&nbsp;</sub>- v<sub>GS</sub> plane, the above equation corresponds to a line with a slope of -1/R<sub>S</sub> that intercepts the horizontal axis at a v<sub>GS</sub> value given by Equation 3. Returning to our example, we obtain the graphical representation below in Figure 16.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/uploads/thumbnails/jfet_biasing_techniques_figure_16.png" /></p>
<h5><em><strong>Figure 16. </strong>Bias point curves for the JFET circuit with voltage divider biasing.</em></h5>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The above figure shows two possible bias lines that have different slopes and intercept points, A<sub>1</sub> and A<sub>2</sub>. In fact, if we aim to have a particular bias line, we can choose the appropriate R<sub>S</sub> value to provide the required slope; and then choose parameters, R<sub>1</sub> and R<sub>2</sub>, to adjust the x-axis intercept point. In the next section of this series, we&rsquo;ll continue this discussion and investigate bias sensitivity to parameter variations.</p>
