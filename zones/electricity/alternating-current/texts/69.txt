<h1>Practical Power Factor Correction</h1>
<p>When the need arises to correct for poor power factor in an <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-4/introduction-to-ac-circuits/" target="_blank">AC power system</a>, you probably won&rsquo;t have the luxury of knowing the load&rsquo;s exact inductance in henrys to use for your calculations.</p>
<p>You may be fortunate enough to have an instrument called a <em>power factor meter</em> to tell you what the power factor is (a number between 0 and 1), and the apparent power (which can be figured by taking a voltmeter reading in volts and multiplying by an ammeter reading in amps).</p>
<p>In less favorable circumstances, you may have to use an oscilloscope to compare <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">voltage and current</a> waveforms, measuring <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-1/ac-phase/" target="_blank">phase shift</a> in <em>degrees</em> and calculating power factor by the cosine of that phase shift.</p>
<p>Most likely, you will have access to a wattmeter for measuring true power, whose reading you can compare against a calculation of apparent power (from multiplying total voltage and total current measurements). From the values of true and apparent power, you can determine reactive power and power factor.</p>

<h3>How to Correct Power Factor with a Capacitor</h3>
<p>If this load is an electric motor or most any other industrial AC load, it will have a lagging (inductive) power factor, which means that we&rsquo;ll have to correct for it with a <em>capacitor</em> of appropriate size, wired in parallel.</p>
<p>Now that we know the amount of reactive power (1.754 kVAR), we can calculate the size of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-13/practical-considerations-capacitors/" target="_blank">capacitor</a> needed to counteract its effects:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/correct-power-factor-with-capacitor-equation.png" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Rounding this answer off to 80 &micro;F, we can place that size of the capacitor in the circuit and calculate the results: (Figure below)</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/parallel-capacitor-corrects-lagging.png" /></p>
<p><em>Parallel capacitor corrects lagging (inductive) load.</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/capacitor-reactive-power-subtract-from-load-reactive-power.png" /></p>
