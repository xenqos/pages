# Three-phase Transformer Circuits
<p>Since three-phase is used so often for power distribution systems, it makes sense that we would need three-phase transformers to be able to step voltages up or down.</p>
<p>This is only partially true, as regular single-phase transformers can be ganged together to transform power between two three-phase systems in a variety of configurations, eliminating the requirement for a special three-phase transformer.</p>
<p>However, special three-phase transformers are built for those tasks and are able to perform with less material requirement, less size, and less weight than their modular counterparts.</p>
## Three-Phase Transformer Windings and Connections
<p>A three-phase transformer is made of three sets of primary and secondary windings, each set wound around one leg of an iron core assembly. Essentially it looks like three single-phase transformers sharing a joined core as in Figure below.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/three-phase-transformer-core.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Three phase transformer core has three sets of windings.</em></p>

<p>Those sets of primary and secondary windings will be connected in either &Delta; or Y configurations to form a complete unit. The various combinations of ways that these windings can be connected together it will be the focus of this section.</p>
<p>Whether the winding sets share a common core assembly or each winding pair is a separate transformer, the winding connection options are the same:</p>

<p><strong>Primary - Secondary</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Y - Y</li>
<li>Y - &Delta;</li>
<li>&Delta; - Y</li>
<li>&Delta; - &Delta;</li>
</ul>

<p>The reasons for choosing a Y or &Delta; configuration for <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-9/practical-considerations-transformers/" target="_blank">transformer</a> winding connections are the same as for any other three-phase application: Y connections provide the opportunity for multiple voltages, while &Delta; connections enjoy a higher level of reliability (if one winding fails open, the other two can still maintain full line voltages to the load).</p>
<p>Probably the most important aspect of connecting three sets of primary and secondary windings together to form a three-phase transformer bank is paying attention to proper winding phasing (the dots used to denote &ldquo;polarity&rdquo; of windings).</p>
<p>Remember the proper phase relationships between the phase windings of &Delta; and Y: (Figure below)</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-windings-relationship.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>(Y) The center point of the &ldquo;Y&rdquo; must tie either all the &ldquo;-&rdquo; or all the &ldquo;+&rdquo; winding points together. (&Delta;) The winding polarities must stack together in a complementary manner ( + to -).</em></p>

<p>Getting this phasing correct when the windings aren&rsquo;t shown in regular Y or &Delta; configuration can be tricky. Let me illustrate, starting with the figure below.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example-1.png" /></p>
<p><em>Inputs A<sub>1</sub>, B<sub>1</sub>, C<sub>1</sub> may be wired either &ldquo;&Delta;&rdquo; or &ldquo;Y&rdquo;, as may outputs A<sub>2</sub>, B<sub>2</sub>, C<sub>2</sub>.</em></p>

### Phase Wiring for &ldquo;Y-Y&rdquo; Transformer
<p>Three individual transformers are to be connected together to transform power from one three-phase system to another. First, I&rsquo;ll show the wiring connections for a Y-Y configuration:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example2.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Phase wiring for &ldquo;Y-Y&rdquo; transformer.</em></p>

<p>Note in Figure above how all the winding ends marked with dots are connected to their respective phases A, B, and C, while the non-dot ends are connected together to form the centers of each &ldquo;Y&rdquo;.</p>
<p>Having both primary and secondary winding sets connected in &ldquo;Y&rdquo; formations allows for the use of neutral conductors (N<sub>1</sub> and N<sub>2</sub>) in each power system.</p>
### Phase Wiring for &ldquo;Y-&Delta;&rdquo; Transformer
<p>Now, we&rsquo;ll take a look at a Y-&Delta; configuration:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example3.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Phase wiring for &ldquo;Y-&Delta;&rdquo; transformer.</em></p>

<p>Note how the secondary windings (bottom set, Figure above) are connected in a chain, the &ldquo;dot&rdquo; side of one winding connected to the &ldquo;non-dot&rdquo; side of the next, forming the &Delta; loop.</p>
<p>At every connection point between pairs of windings, a connection is made to a line of the second power system (A, B, and C).</p>
### Phase Wiring for &ldquo;&Delta;-Y&rdquo; Transformer
<p>Now, let&rsquo;s examine a &Delta;-Y system in the figure below.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example4.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Phase wiring for &ldquo;&Delta;-Y&rdquo; transformer.</em></p>

<p>Such a configuration (Figure above) would allow for the provision of multiple voltages (line-to-line or line-to-neutral) in the second power system, from a source power system having no neutral.</p>
### Phase Wiring for &ldquo;&Delta;-&Delta;&rdquo; Transformer
<p>And finally, we turn to the &Delta;-&Delta; configuration:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example5.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Phase wiring for &ldquo;&Delta;-&Delta;&rdquo; transformer.</em></p>

<p>When there is no need for a neutral conductor in the secondary power system, &Delta;-&Delta; connection schemes (Figure above) are preferred because of the inherent reliability of the &Delta; configuration.</p>
### Phase Wiring for &ldquo;V&rdquo; or &ldquo;open-&Delta;&rdquo; Transformer
<p>Considering that a &Delta; configuration can operate satisfactorily missing one winding, some power system designers choose to create a three-phase transformer bank with only two transformers, representing a &Delta;-&Delta; configuration with a missing winding in both the primary and secondary sides:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/phase-wiring-for-transformer-example6.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>&ldquo;V&rdquo; or &ldquo;open-&Delta;&rdquo; provides 2-&phi; power with only two transformers.</em></p>

<p>This configuration is called &ldquo;V&rdquo; or &ldquo;Open-&Delta;.&rdquo; Of course, each of the two transformers has to be oversized to handle the same amount of power as three in a standard &Delta; configuration, but the overall size, weight, and cost advantages are often worth it.</p>
<p>Bear in mind, however, that with one winding set missing from the &Delta; shape, this system no longer provides the fault tolerance of a normal &Delta;-&Delta; system. If one of the two transformers were to fail, the load voltage and current would definitely be affected.</p>
### Real Life Example
<p>The following photograph (figure below) shows a bank of step-up transformers at the Grand Coulee hydroelectric dam in Washington state.</p>
<p>Several transformers (green in color) may be seen from this vantage point, and they have grouped in threes: three transformers per hydroelectric generator, wired together in some form of three-phase configuration.</p>
<p>The photograph doesn&rsquo;t reveal the primary winding connections, but it appears the secondaries are connected in a Y configuration, is that there is only one large high-voltage <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/conductors-insulators-electron-flow/" target="_blank">insulator</a> protruding from each transformer.</p>
<p>This suggests the other side of each transformer&rsquo;s secondary winding is at or near ground potential, which could only be true in a Y system.</p>
