## Voltage and Current Values in Three-Phase Systems
<p>When we measure voltage and current in three-phase systems, we need to be specific as to <em>where</em> we&rsquo;re measuring.</p>
<p><em>Line voltage</em> refers to the amount of voltage measured between any two line conductors in a balanced three-phase system. With the above circuit, the line voltage is roughly 208 volts.</p>
<p><em>Phase voltage</em> refers to the voltage measured across any one component (source winding or load impedance) in a balanced three-phase source or load.</p>
<p>For the circuit shown above, the phase voltage is 120 volts. The terms <em>line current</em> and <em>phase current</em> follows the same logic: the former referring to the current through any one line conductor, and the latter to the current through any one component.</p>
<p>Y-connected sources and loads always have line voltages greater than phase voltages, and line currents equal to phase currents. If the Y-connected source or load is balanced, the line voltage will be equal to the phase voltage times the square root of 3:</p>

<p>However, the &ldquo;Y&rdquo; configuration is not the only valid one for connecting three-phase voltage source or load elements together.</p>
## Three-Phase Delta(&Delta;) Configuration
<p>Another configuration is known as the &ldquo;Delta,&rdquo; for its geometric resemblance to the Greek letter of the same name (&Delta;). Take close notice of the polarity for each winding in the figure below.</p>

^[62-05.webp]
<p><em>Three-phase, three-wire &Delta; connection has no common.</em></p>

<p>At first glance, it seems as though three voltage sources like this would create a short-circuit, electrons flowing around the triangle with nothing but the internal impedance of the windings to hold them back.</p>
<p>Due to the phase angles of these three voltage sources, however, this is not the case.</p>

## Advantages of the Delta Three-Phase System
<p>So each line current in this three-phase power system is equal to 144.34 amps, which is substantially more than the line currents in the Y-connected system we looked at earlier.</p>
<p>One might wonder if we&rsquo;ve lost all the advantages of three-phase power here, given the fact that we have such greater conductor currents, necessitating thicker, more costly wire.</p>
<p>The answer is no. Although this circuit would require three number 1 gauge copper conductors (at 1000 feet of distance between source and load this equates to a little over 750 pounds of copper for the whole system), it is still less than the 1000+ pounds of copper required for a single-phase system delivering the same power (30 kW) at the same voltage (120 volts conductor-to-conductor).</p>
<p>One distinct advantage of a &Delta;-connected system is its lack of a neutral wire. With a Y-connected system, a neutral wire was needed in case one of the phase loads were to fail open (or be turned off), in order to keep the phase voltages at the load from changing.</p>
<p>This is not necessary (or even possible!) in a &Delta;-connected circuit.</p>
<p>With each load phase element directly connected across a respective source phase winding, the phase voltage will be constant regardless of open failures in the load elements.</p>
<p>Perhaps the greatest advantage of the &Delta;-connected source is its fault tolerance.</p>
<p>It is possible for one of the windings in a &Delta;-connected three-phase source to fail open (Figure below) without affecting load voltage or current!</p>

^[62-12.webp]
<p><em>Even with a source winding failure, the line voltage is still 120 V, and the load phase voltage is still 120 V. The only difference is extra current in the remaining functional source windings.</em></p>

<p>The only consequence of a source winding failing open for a &Delta;-connected source is increased phase current in the remaining windings. Compare this fault tolerance with a Y-connected system suffering an open source winding in the figure below.</p>

^[62-13.webp]
<p><em>Open &ldquo;Y&rdquo; source winding halves the voltage on two loads of a &Delta; connected the load.</em></p>

<p>With a &Delta;-connected load, two of the resistances suffer reduced voltage while one remains at the original line voltage, 208. A Y-connected load suffers an even worse fate (Figure below) with the same winding failure in a Y-connected source.</p>

^[62-14.webp]
<p><em>Open source winding of a &ldquo;Y-Y&rdquo; system halves the voltage on two loads and loses one load entirely.</em></p>

<p>In this case, two load resistances suffer reduced voltage while the third loses supply voltage completely! For this reason, &Delta;-connected sources are preferred for reliability.</p>
<p>However, if dual voltages are needed (e.g. 120/208) or preferred for lower line currents, Y-connected systems are the configuration of choice.</p>

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The conductors connected to the three points of a three-phase source or load are called <em>lines</em>.</li>
<li>The three components comprising a three-phase source or load are called <em>phases</em>.</li>
<li><em>Line voltage</em> is the voltage measured between any two lines in a three-phase circuit.</li>
<li><em>Phase voltage</em> is the voltage measured across a single component in a three-phase source or load.</li>
<li><em>Line current</em> is the current through any one line between a three-phase source and load.</li>
<li><em>Phase current</em> is the current through any one component comprising a three-phase source or load.</li>
<li>In balanced &ldquo;Y&rdquo; circuits, the line voltage is equal to phase voltage times the square root of 3, while the line current is equal to phase current.</li>
</ul>
^[62-15.webp]
<ul>
<li>In balanced &Delta; circuits, the line voltage is equal to phase voltage, while the line current is equal to phase current times the square root of 3.</li>
</ul>
^[62-16.webp]
<ul>
<li>&Delta;-connected three-phase voltage sources give greater reliability in the event of winding failure than Y-connected sources. However, Y-connected sources can deliver the same amount of power with less line current than &Delta;-connected sources.</li>
</ul>
