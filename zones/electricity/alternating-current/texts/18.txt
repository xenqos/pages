# Parallel Resistor-Inductor Circuits
<p>Let&rsquo;s take the same components for our series example circuit and connect them in parallel:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/parallel-r-l-circuit.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Parallel R-L circuit.</em></p>

<p>Because the power source has the same frequency as the series example circuit, and the resistor and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/inductors-part-1/" target="_blank">inductor</a> both have the same values of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/resistance/" target="_blank">resistance</a> and inductance, respectively, they must also have the same values of impedance. So, we can begin our analysis table with the same &ldquo;given&rdquo; values:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/impedance-analysis-table1.jpg" /></p>

<p>The only difference in our analysis technique this time is that we will apply the rules of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">parallel circuits</a> instead of the rules for series circuits. The approach is fundamentally the same as for DC. We know that voltage is shared uniformly by all components in a parallel circuit, so we can transfer the figure of total voltage (10 volts &ang; 0&deg;) to all component columns:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/impedance-analysis-table2.jpg" /></p>

<p>Now we can apply <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/voltage-current-resistance-relate/" target="_blank">Ohm&rsquo;s Law</a> (I=E/Z) vertically to two columns of the table, calculating current through the resistor and current through the inductor:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/impedance-analysis-table3.jpg" /></p>

<p>Just as with DC circuits, branch currents in a parallel AC circuit add to form the total current (<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/kirchhoffs-current-law-kcl/" target="_blank">Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Current Law</a> still holds true for AC as it did for DC):</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/impedance-analysis-table4.jpg" /></p>

<p>Finally, total impedance can be calculated by using Ohm&rsquo;s Law (Z=E/I) vertically in the &ldquo;Total&rdquo; column. Incidentally, parallel impedance can also be calculated by using a reciprocal formula identical to that used in calculating <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">parallel resistances</a>.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/parallel-resistances-formula.jpg" /></p>

<p>The only problem with using this formula is that it typically involves a lot of calculator keystrokes to carry out.</p>
<p>And if you&rsquo;re determined to run through a formula like this &ldquo;longhand,&rdquo; be prepared for a very large amount of work! But, just as with DC circuits, we often have multiple options in calculating the quantities in our analysis tables, and this example is no different.</p>
<p>No matter which way you calculate total impedance (Ohm&rsquo;s Law or the reciprocal formula), you will arrive at the same figure:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/impedance-analysis-table5.jpg" /></p>

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Impedances (Z) are managed just like resistances (R) in parallel circuit analysis: parallel impedances diminish to form the total impedance, using the reciprocal formula. Just be sure to perform all calculations in complex (not scalar) form! ZTotal = 1/(1/Z1 + 1/Z2 + . . . 1/Zn)</li>
<li>Ohm&rsquo;s Law for AC circuits: E = IZ ; I = E/Z ; Z = E/I</li>
<li>When resistors and inductors are mixed together in parallel circuits (just as in series circuits), the total impedance will have a phase angle somewhere between 0&deg; and +90&deg;. The circuit current will have a phase angle somewhere between 0&deg; and -90&deg;.</li>
<li>Parallel AC circuits exhibit the same fundamental properties as parallel DC circuits: voltage is uniform throughout the circuit, branch currents add to form the total current, and impedances diminish (through the reciprocal formula) to form the total impedance.</li>
</ul>
