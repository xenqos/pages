###### 04. Reactance and Impedance—Capacitive

# AC Capacitor Circuits

## Capacitors Vs. Resistors
<p>Capacitors do not behave the same as resistors. Whereas resistors allow a flow of electrons through them directly proportional to the voltage drop, capacitors oppose <em>changes</em> in voltage by drawing or supplying current as they charge or discharge to the new voltage level.</p>
<p>The flow of electrons &ldquo;through&rdquo; a capacitor is directly proportional to the <em>rate of change</em> of voltage across the capacitor. This opposition to voltage change is another form of <em>reactance</em>, but one that is precisely opposite to the kind exhibited by inductors.</p>

<p>If we were to plot the current and voltage for this very simple circuit, it would look something like this:</p>
^[22-03.webp]

<p>Remember, the current through a capacitor is a reaction against the <em>change</em> in voltage across it.</p>
<p>Therefore, the instantaneous current is zero whenever the instantaneous voltage is at a peak (zero change, or level slope, on the voltage sine wave), and the instantaneous current is at a peak wherever the instantaneous voltage is at maximum change (the points of steepest slope on the voltage wave, where it crosses the zero line).</p>
<p>This results in a voltage wave that is -90&deg; out of phase with the current wave. Looking at the graph, the current wave seems to have a &ldquo;head start&rdquo; on the voltage wave; the current &ldquo;leads&rdquo; the voltage, and the voltage &ldquo;lags&rdquo; behind the current.</p>

^[22-04.webp]
<p><em>Voltage lags current by 90&deg; in a pure capacitive circuit.</em></p>

<p>As you might have guessed, the same unusual power wave that we saw with the simple inductor circuit is present in the simple capacitor circuit, too:</p>

^[22-05.webp]
<p><em>In a pure capacitive circuit, the instantaneous power may be positive or negative.</em></p>

<p>As with the simple inductor circuit, the 90-degree phase shift between voltage and current results in a power wave that alternates equally between positive and negative. This means that a capacitor does not dissipate power as it reacts against changes in voltage; it merely absorbs and releases power, alternately.</p>
## A Capacitor&rsquo;s Reactance
<p>A capacitor&rsquo;s opposition to change in voltage translates to an opposition to alternating voltage in general, which is by definition always changing in instantaneous magnitude and direction.</p>
<p>For any given magnitude of AC voltage at a given frequency, a capacitor of given size will &ldquo;conduct&rdquo; a certain magnitude of AC current.</p>
<p>Just as the current through a resistor is a function of the voltage across the resistor and the resistance offered by the resistor, the AC current through a capacitor is a function of the AC voltage across it, and the <em>reactance</em> offered by the capacitor.</p>
<p>As with inductors, the reactance of a capacitor is expressed in ohms and symbolized by the letter X (or XC to be more specific).</p>
<p>Since capacitors &ldquo;conduct&rdquo; current in proportion to the rate of voltage change, they will pass more current for faster-changing voltages (as they charge and discharge to the same voltage peaks in less time), and less current for slower-changing voltages.</p>
<p>What this means is that reactance in ohms for any capacitor is <em>inversely</em> proportional to the frequency of the alternating current.</p>

<p>Please note that the relationship of capacitive reactance to frequency is exactly opposite from that of inductive reactance.</p>
<p>Capacitive reactance (in ohms) decreases with increasing AC frequency. Conversely, inductive reactance (in ohms) increases with increasing AC frequency. Inductors oppose faster changing currents by producing greater voltage drops; capacitors oppose faster changing voltage drops by allowing greater currents.</p>
<p>As with inductors, the reactance equation&rsquo;s 2&pi;f term may be replaced by the lowercase Greek letter Omega (&omega;), which is referred to as the <em>angular velocity</em> of the AC circuit. Thus, the equation XC = 1/(2&pi;fC) could also be written as XC = 1/(&omega;C), with &omega; cast in units of <em>radians per second</em>.</p>
<p>Alternating current in a simple capacitive circuit is equal to the voltage (in volts) divided by the capacitive reactance (in ohms), just as either alternating or direct current in a simple resistive circuit is equal to the voltage (in volts) divided by the resistance (in ohms). <p>Mathematically, we say that the phase angle of a capacitor&rsquo;s opposition to current is -90&deg;, meaning that a capacitor&rsquo;s opposition to current is a negative imaginary quantity. (See figure above.) This phase angle of reactive opposition to current becomes critically important in circuit analysis, especially for complex AC circuits where reactance and resistance interact.</p>
<p>It will prove beneficial to represent <em>any</em> component&rsquo;s opposition to current in terms of complex numbers, and not just scalar quantities of resistance and reactance.</p>

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><em>Capacitive reactance</em> is the opposition that a capacitor offers to alternating current due to its phase-shifted storage and release of energy in its electric field. Reactance is symbolized by the capital letter &ldquo;X&rdquo; and is measured in ohms just like resistance (R).</li>
<li>Capacitive reactance can be calculated using this formula: XC = 1/(2&pi;fC)</li>
<li>Capacitive reactance <em>decreases</em> with increasing frequency. In other words, the higher the frequency, the less it opposes (the more it &ldquo;conducts&rdquo;) AC current.</li>
</ul>
