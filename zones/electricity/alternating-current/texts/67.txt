# True, Reactive, and Apparent Power
## Reactive Power
<p>We know that reactive loads such as inductors and capacitors dissipate zero power, yet the fact that they drop voltage and draw current gives the deceptive impression that they actually <em>do</em> dissipate power.</p>
<p>This &ldquo;phantom power&rdquo; is called <em>reactive power</em>, and it is measured in a unit called <em>Volt-Amps-Reactive</em> (VAR), rather than watts.</p>
<p>The mathematical symbol for reactive power is (unfortunately) the capital letter Q.</p>

## True Power
<p>The actual amount of power being used, or dissipated, in a circuit is called <em>true power</em>, and it is measured in watts (symbolized by the capital letter P, as always).</p>

## Apparent Power
<p>The combination of reactive power and true power is called <em>apparent power</em>, and it is the product of a circuit&rsquo;s voltage and current, without reference to phase angle.</p>
<p>Apparent power is measured in the unit of <em>Volt-Amps</em> (VA) and is symbolized by the capital letter S.</p>

## Calculating for Reactive, True, or Apparent Power
<p>As a rule, true power is a function of a circuit&rsquo;s dissipative elements, usually resistances (R). Reactive power is a function of a circuit&rsquo;s reactance (X).</p>
<p>Apparent power is a function of a circuit&rsquo;s total impedance (Z). Since we&rsquo;re dealing with scalar quantities for power calculation, any complex starting quantities such as voltage (V), current (I), and impedance must be represented by their <em>polar magnitudes</em>, not by real or imaginary rectangular components.</p>
<p>For instance, if I&rsquo;m calculating true power from current and resistance, I must use the polar magnitude for current, and not merely the &ldquo;real&rdquo; or &ldquo;imaginary&rdquo; portion of the current.</p>
<p>If I&rsquo;m calculating apparent power from voltage and impedance, both of these formerly complex quantities must be reduced to their polar magnitudes for the scalar arithmetic.</p>

### Equations Using Scalar Quantities
<p>There are several power equations relating the three types of power to resistance, reactance, and impedance (all using scalar quantities):</p>
<p>$$\text{True Power in Watts} = P = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$</p>
<p>$$\text{Reactive Power in Volt-Amps-Reactive (VAR)} = Q = I^2X = \frac{V^2}{X}$$</p>
<p>$$\text{Apparent Power in Volt-Amps (VAR)} = S = I^2Z = \frac{V^2}{Z} = IV$$</p>

<p>Please note that there are two equations each for the calculation of true and reactive power.&nbsp;There are three equations available for the calculation of apparent power, <em>P</em>=<em>IV</em> being useful <em>only</em> for that purpose.</p>

## Review
<ul>
<li>The power dissipated by a load is referred to as <em>true power</em>. True power is symbolized by the letter P and is measured in the unit of Watts (W).</li>
<li>Power merely absorbed and returned in load due to its reactive properties is referred to as <em>reactive power</em>. Reactive power is symbolized by the letter Q and is measured in the unit of Volt-Amps-Reactive (VAR).</li>
<li>Total power in an AC circuit, both dissipated and absorbed/returned is referred to as <em>apparent power</em>. Apparent power is symbolized by the letter S and is measured in the unit of Volt-Amps (VA).</li>
<li>These three types of power are trigonometrically related to one another. In a right triangle, P = adjacent length, Q = opposite length, and S = hypotenuse length. The opposite angle is equal to the circuit&rsquo;s impedance (Z) phase angle.</li>
</ul>
