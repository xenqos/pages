# What Is the Skin Effect?
## The Skin Depth of Copper in Electrical Engineering
<p>As previously mentioned, the skin effect is where alternating current tends to avoid travel through the center of a solid conductor, limiting itself to conduction near the surface.</p>
<p>This effectively limits the cross-sectional conductor area available to carry alternating electron flow, increasing the resistance of that conductor above what it would normally be for direct current:</p>

^[20-01.webp]
<p><em>Skin effect: skin depth decreases with increasing frequency.</em></p>

<p>The electrical resistance of the conductor with all its cross-sectional area in use is known as the &ldquo;DC resistance.&rdquo; The &ldquo;AC resistance&rdquo; of the same conductor refers to a higher figure resulting from the skin effect.</p>
<p>As you can see, at high frequencies the AC current avoids traveling through most of the conductor&rsquo;s cross-sectional area. For the purpose of conducting current, the wire might as well be hollow!</p>
## Hollow Conductors in RF Applications
<p>In some radio applications (antennas, most notably) this effect is exploited. Since radio-frequency (&ldquo;RF&rdquo;) AC currents wouldn&rsquo;t travel through the middle of a conductor anyway, why not just use hollow metal rods instead of solid metal wires and save both weight and cost? (See figure below.)</p>
<p>Most antenna structures and RF power conductors are made of hollow metal tubes for this reason.</p>
<p>In the following photograph, you can see some large inductors used in a 50 kW radio transmitting circuit. The inductors are hollow copper tubes coated with silver, for excellent conductivity at the &ldquo;skin&rdquo; of the tube:</p>
