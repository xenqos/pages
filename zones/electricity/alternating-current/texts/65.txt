# Harmonic Phase Sequences
<p>In the last section, we saw how the 3rd harmonic and all of its integer multiples (collectively called <em>triplen</em> harmonics) generated by 120&deg; phase-shifted fundamental waveforms are actually in phase with each other.</p>
<p>In a 60 Hz three-phase power system, where phases <strong>A</strong>, <strong>B</strong>, and <strong>C</strong> are 120&deg; apart, the third-harmonic multiples of those frequencies (180 Hz) fall perfectly into phase with each other.</p>
<p>This can be thought of in graphical terms, (figure below) and/or in mathematical terms:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/harmonic-currents-of-phases.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Harmonic currents of Phases A, B, C all coincide, that is, no rotation.</em></p>

<p><em>Extended Mathematical Table with Odd-Numbered Harmonics</em></p>

<p>If we extend the mathematical table to include higher odd-numbered harmonics, we will notice an interesting pattern develop with regard to the rotation or sequence of the harmonic frequencies:</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/articles/extended-mathematical-table-with-odd-numbered-harmonics.jpg" /></p>

<p>Harmonics such as the 7th, which &ldquo;rotate&rdquo; with the same sequence as the fundamental, are called <em>positive sequence</em>.</p>
<p>Harmonics such as the 5th, which &ldquo;rotate&rdquo; in the opposite sequence as the fundamental, are called <em>negative sequence</em>.</p>
<p>Triplen harmonics (3rd and 9th shown in this table) which don&rsquo;t &ldquo;rotate&rdquo; at all because they&rsquo;re in phase with each other, are called <em>zero sequence</em>.</p>
<p>This pattern of positive-zero-negative-positive continues indefinitely for all odd-numbered harmonics, lending itself to expression in a table like this:</p>
