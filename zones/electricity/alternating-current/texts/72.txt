<h1>Power Measurement</h1>
<p>Power measurement in AC circuits can be quite a bit more complex than with DC circuits for the simple reason that phase shift complicates the matter beyond multiplying the voltage by current figures obtained with meters.</p>
<p>What is needed is an instrument able to determine the product (multiplication) of <em>instantaneous</em> voltage and current. Fortunately, the common electrodynamometer movement with its stationary and moving coil does a fine job of this.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-10/three-phase-power-systems/" target="_blank">Three phase</a> power measurement can be accomplished using two dynamometer movements with a common shaft linking the two moving coils together so that a single pointer registers power on a meter movement scale. This, obviously, makes for a rather expensive and complex movement mechanism, but it is a workable solution.</p>
<h3>Hall Effect</h3>
<p>An ingenious method of deriving an electronic power meter (one that generates an electric signal representing power in the system rather than merely moves a pointer) is based on the Hall effect.</p>
<p>The Hall effect is an unusual effect first noticed by E. H. Hall in 1879, whereby a voltage is generated along the width of a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/wire-and-cable/" target="_blank">current-carrying conductor</a> exposed to a perpendicular&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-15/magnetic-fields-and-inductance/" target="_blank">magnetic field</a>:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/hall-effect.png" /></p>
<p><em>Hall effect: Voltage is proportional to current and strength of the perpendicular magnetic field.</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The voltage generated across the width of the flat, rectangular conductor is directly proportional to both the magnitude of the current through it and the strength of the magnetic field.</p>
<p>Mathematically, it is a product (multiplication) of these two variables. The amount of &ldquo;Hall Voltage&rdquo; produced for any given set of conditions also depends on the type of material used for the flat, rectangular conductor.</p>
<p>It has been found that specially prepared &ldquo;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/" target="_blank">semiconductor</a>&rdquo; materials produce a greater Hall voltage than do metals, and so modern Hall Effect devices are made of these.</p>
<p>It makes sense then that if we were to build a device using a Hall-effect sensor where the current through the conductor was pushed by AC voltage from an external circuit and the magnetic field was set up by a pair of wire coils energized by the current of the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-11/power-resistive-reactive-ac-circuits/" target="_blank">AC power</a> circuit, the Hall voltage would be in direct proportion to the multiple of circuit current and voltage.</p>
<p>Having no mass to move (unlike an electromechanical movement), this device is able to provide <em>instantaneous</em> power measurement:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/hall-effect-power-sensor.jpg" /></p>
<p><em>Hall effect power sensor measures instantaneous power.</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Not only will the output voltage of the Hall effect device be the representation of instantaneous power at any point in time, but it will also be a DC signal! This is because the Hall voltage polarity is dependent upon <em>both</em> the polarity of the magnetic field and the direction of current through the conductor.</p>
<p>If both current direction and magnetic field polarity reverse&mdash;as it would ever half-cycle of the AC power&mdash;the output voltage polarity will stay the same.</p>
<p>If&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/electrical-quantities-a/" target="_blank">voltage and current</a> in the power circuit are 90&deg; out of phase (a power factor of zero, meaning <em>no</em> real power delivered to the load), the alternate peaks of Hall device current and magnetic field will never coincide with each other: when one is at its peak, the other will be zero.</p>
<p>At those points in time, the Hall output voltage will likewise be zero, being the product (multiplication) of current and magnetic field strength.</p>
<p>Between those points in time, the Hall output voltage will fluctuate equally between positive and negative, generating a signal corresponding to the instantaneous absorption and release of power through the reactive load.</p>
<p>The net DC output voltage will be zero, indicating zero true power in the circuit.</p>
<p>Any phase shift between voltage and current in the power circuit less than 90&deg; will result in a Hall output voltage that oscillates between positive and negative but spends more time positive than negative. Consequently, there will be a net DC output voltage.</p>
<p>Conditioned through a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-8/low-pass-filters/" target="_blank">low-pass filter circuit</a>, this net DC voltage can be separated from the AC mixed with it, the final output signal registered on a sensitive DC meter movement.</p>
<p>Often it is useful to have a meter to totalize power usage over a period of time rather than instantaneously. The output of such a meter can be set in units of Joules, or total energy consumed since <em>power</em> is a measure of work being done <em>per</em> unit time.</p>
<p>Or, more commonly, the output of the meter can be set in units of Watt-Hours.</p>
