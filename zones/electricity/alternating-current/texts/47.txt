<h1>Band-stop Filters</h1>
<h3>How to Create a Band-stop Filter?</h3>
<p>Also called <em>band-elimination</em>, <em>band-reject</em>, or <em>notch</em> filters, this kind of filter passes all frequencies above and below a particular range set by the component values. Not surprisingly, it can be made out of a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-8/low-pass-filters/" target="_blank">low-pass</a> and a&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-8/high-pass-filters/" target="_blank">high-pass</a> filter, just like the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/alternating-current/chpt-8/band-pass-filters/" target="_blank">band-pass</a> design, except that this time we connect the two filter sections in parallel with each other instead of in series.</p>

<p>Constructed using two capacitive filter sections, it looks something like:<strong> </strong></p>

<p>The low-pass filter section is comprised of R<sub>1</sub>, R<sub>2</sub>, and C<sub>1</sub> in a &ldquo;T&rdquo; configuration. The high-pass filter section is comprised of C<sub>2</sub>, C<sub>3</sub>, and R<sub>3</sub> in a &ldquo;T&rdquo; configuration as well. Together, this arrangement is commonly known as a &ldquo;Twin-T&rdquo; filter, giving sharp response when the component values are chosen in the following ratios:</p>

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A <em>band-stop</em> filter works to screen out frequencies that are within a certain range, giving easy passage only to frequencies outside of that range. Also known as <em>band-elimination</em>, <em>band-reject</em>, or <em>notch</em> filters.</li>
<li>Band-stop filters can be made by placing a low-pass filter in parallel with a high-pass filter. Commonly, both the low-pass and high-pass filter sections are of the &ldquo;T&rdquo; configuration, giving the name &ldquo;Twin-T&rdquo; to the band-stop combination.</li>
<li>The frequency of maximum attenuation is called the <em>notch</em> frequency.</li>
</ul>
