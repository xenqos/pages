###### 15. Inductors

# Factors Affecting Inductance
<p>There are four basic factors of inductor construction determining the amount of inductance created. These factors all dictate inductance by affecting how much magnetic field flux will develop for a given amount of magnetic field force (current through the inductor&rsquo;s wire coil):</p>
## Number of Wire Wraps, or &ldquo;Turns&rdquo; in the Coil
<p>All other factors being equal, a greater number of turns of wire in the coil results in greater inductance; fewer turns of wire in the coil results in less inductance.</p>
<p><em>Explanation:</em> More turns of wire means that the coil will generate a greater amount of magnetic field force (measured in amp-turns!), for a given amount of coil current.</p>

^[111-01.png]

## Coil Area
<p>All other factors being equal, greater coil area (as measured looking lengthwise through the coil, at the cross-section of the core) results in greater inductance; less coil area results in less inductance.</p>
<p><em>Explanation:</em> Greater coil area presents less opposition to the formation of magnetic field flux, for a given amount of field force (amp-turns).</p>

^[111-02.png]

## Coil Length
<p>All other factors being equal, the longer the coil&rsquo;s length, the less inductance; the shorter the coil&rsquo;s length, the greater the inductance.</p>
<p><em>Explanation:</em> A longer path for the magnetic field flux to take results in more opposition to the formation of that flux for any given amount of field force (amp-turns).</p>

^[111-03.png]

## Core Material
<p>All other factors being equal, the greater the magnetic permeability of the core which the coil is wrapped around, the greater the inductance; the less the permeability of the core, the less the inductance.</p>
<p><em>Explanation:</em> A core material with greater magnetic permeability results in greater magnetic field flux for any given amount of field force (amp-turns).</p>

<p>It must be understood that this formula yields <em>approximate</em> figures only. One reason for this is the fact that permeability changes as the field intensity varies (remember the nonlinear &ldquo;B-H&rdquo; curves for different materials). Obviously, if permeability (&micro;) in the equation is unstable, then the inductance (L) will also be unstable to some degree as the current through the coil changes in magnitude.</p>
<p>If the hysteresis of the core material is significant, this will also have strange effects on the inductance of the coil. Inductor designers try to minimize these effects by designing the core in such a way that its flux density never approaches saturation levels, and so the inductor operates in a more linear portion of the B/H curve.</p>
<p>If an inductor is designed so that any one of these factors may be varied at will, its inductance will correspondingly vary.
