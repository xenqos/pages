<h1>Component Failure Analysis (Continued)</h1>
<blockquote><em>&ldquo;I consider that I understand an equation when I can predict the properties of its solutions, without actually solving it.&rdquo;</em> <strong> &mdash;P.A.M Dirac, physicist</strong></blockquote>
<p>There is a lot of truth to that quote from Dirac. With a little modification, I can extend his wisdom to electric circuits by saying, &ldquo;I consider that I understand a circuit when I can predict the approximate effects of various changes made to it without actually performing any calculations.&rdquo;</p>
<p>At the end of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/series-circuits-part-1/" target="_blank">series</a> and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">parallel</a> circuits chapter, we briefly considered how circuits could be analyzed in a <em>qualitative</em> rather than <em>quantitative</em> manner. Building this skill is an important step towards becoming a proficient troubleshooter of electric circuits. Once you have a thorough understanding of how any particular failure will affect a circuit (i.e. you don&rsquo;t have to perform any arithmetic to predict the results), it will be much easier to work the other way around: pinpointing the source of trouble by assessing how a circuit is behaving.</p>
<p>Also shown at the end of the series and parallel circuits chapter was how the table method works just as well for aiding failure analysis as it does for the analysis of healthy circuits. We may take this technique one step further and adapt it for total qualitative analysis. By <em>&ldquo;qualitative&rdquo;</em> I mean working with symbols representing &ldquo;increase,&rdquo; &ldquo;decrease,&rdquo; and &ldquo;same&rdquo; instead of precise numerical figures.</p>
<p>We can still use the principles of series and parallel circuits, and the concepts of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/electrical-quantities-b/" target="_blank">Ohm&rsquo;s Law</a>. We&rsquo;ll just use symbolic <em>qualities </em>instead of numerical <em>quantities</em>. By doing this, we can gain more of an intuitive &ldquo;feel&rdquo; for how circuits work rather than leaning on abstract equations, attaining Dirac&rsquo;s definition of &ldquo;understanding.&rdquo;</p>

Expressed in symbolic form, the total resistance for this circuit is as follows:</p>

<h3>Analysis on Failure Scenario</h3>
<p>Next, we need a failure scenario. Let&rsquo;s suppose that resistor R<sub>2</sub> were to fail shorted. We will assume that all other components maintain their original values. Because we&rsquo;ll be analyzing this circuit qualitatively rather than quantitatively, we won&rsquo;t be inserting any real numbers into the table.</p>
<p>For any quantity unchanged after the component failure, we&rsquo;ll use the word &ldquo;same&rdquo; to represent &ldquo;no change from before.&rdquo; For any quantity that has changed as a result of the failure, we&rsquo;ll use a down arrow for &ldquo;decrease&rdquo; and an up arrow for &ldquo;increase.&rdquo;</p>
<p>As usual, we start by filling in the spaces of the table for individual resistances and total voltage, our &ldquo;given&rdquo; values:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table12.jpg" /></p>
<p>The only &ldquo;given&rdquo; value different from the normal state of the circuit is R<sub>2</sub>, which we said was failed shorted (abnormally low resistance). All other initial values are the same as they were before, as represented by the &ldquo;same&rdquo; entries. All we have to do now is work through the familiar Ohm&rsquo;s Law and series-parallel principles to determine what will happen to all the other circuit values.</p>
<p>First, we need to determine what happens to the resistances of parallel subsections R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub>. If neither R<sub>3</sub> nor R<sub>4</sub> have changed in resistance value, then neither will their parallel combination.</p>
<p>However, since the resistance of R<sub>2</sub> has decreased while R<sub>1</sub> has stayed the same, their parallel combination must decrease in resistance as well:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table3.jpg" /></p>
<p>Now, we need to figure out what happens to the total resistance. This part is easy: when we&rsquo;re dealing with only one component change in the circuit, the change in total resistance will be in the same direction as the change of the failed component. This is not to say that the <em>magnitude</em> of change between individual component and total circuit will be the same, merely the <em>direction</em> of change. In other words, if any single resistor decreases in value, then the total circuit resistance must also decrease, and vice versa.</p>
<p>In this case, since R<sub>2</sub> is the only failed component, and its resistance has decreased, the total resistance <em>must </em>decrease:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table2.jpg" /></p>
<p>Now we can apply Ohm&rsquo;s Law (qualitatively) to the Total column in the table. Given the fact that total voltage has remained the same and total resistance has decreased, we can conclude that total current must increase (I=E/R).</p>
<h3>Using Qualitative Assessment of Ohm&rsquo;s Law in Failure Analysis</h3>
<p>In case you&rsquo;re not familiar with the qualitative assessment of an equation, it works like this. First, we write the equation as solved for the unknown quantity. In this case, we&rsquo;re trying to solve for current, given voltage and resistance:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/ohm-law-equation-one.jpg" /></p>
<p>Now that our equation is in the proper form, we assess what change (if any) will be experienced by &ldquo;I,&rdquo; given the change(s) to &ldquo;E&rdquo; and &ldquo;R&rdquo;:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/ohm-law-equation-two.jpg" /></p>
<p>If the denominator of a fraction decreases in value while the numerator stays the same, then the overall value of the fraction must increase:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/ohm-law-equation-three.jpg" /></p>
<p>Therefore, Ohm&rsquo;s Law (I=E/R) tells us that the current (I) will increase. We&rsquo;ll mark this conclusion in our table with an &ldquo;up&rdquo; arrow:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table7.jpg" /></p>
<p>With all resistance places filled in the table and all quantities determined in the Total column, we can proceed to determine the other voltages and currents. Knowing that the total resistance in this table was the result of R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> in <em>series</em>, we know that the value of total current will be the same as that in R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> (because series components share the same current).</p>
<p>Therefore, if total current increased, then current through R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> must also have increased with the failure of R<sub>2</sub>:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table9.jpg" /></p>
<p>Fundamentally, what we&rsquo;re doing here with a qualitative usage of Ohm&rsquo;s Law and the rules of series and parallel circuits is no different from what we&rsquo;ve done before with numerical figures. In fact, it&rsquo;s a lot easier because you don&rsquo;t have to worry about making an arithmetic or calculator keystroke error in a calculation. Instead, you&rsquo;re just focusing on the <em>principles</em> behind the equations.</p>
<p>From our table above, we can see that Ohm&rsquo;s Law should be applicable to the R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> columns. For R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub>, we figure what happens to the voltage, given an increase in current and no change in resistance. Intuitively, we can see that this must result in an increase in voltage across the parallel combination of R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub>:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table8.jpg" /></p>
<h3>Using the Circuit Analysis Rules on Failure Analysis</h3>
<p>But how do we apply the same Ohm&rsquo;s Law formula (E=IR) to the R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> column, where we have resistance decreasing <em>and</em> current increasing? It&rsquo;s easy to determine if only one variable is changing, as it was with R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub>, but with two variables moving around and no definite numbers to work with, Ohm&rsquo;s Law isn&rsquo;t going to be much help.</p>
<p>However, there is another rule we can apply <em>horizontally</em> to determine what happens to the voltage across R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub>: the rule for voltage in series circuits. If the voltages across R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> add up to equal the total (battery) voltage and we know that the R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> voltage has increased while total voltage has stayed the same, then the voltage across R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> <em>must</em> have decreased with the change of R<sub>2</sub>&lsquo;s resistance value:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table4.jpg" /></p>
<p>Now we&rsquo;re ready to proceed to some new columns in the table. Knowing that R<sub>3</sub> and R<sub>4</sub> comprise the parallel subsection R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub>, and knowing that voltage is shared equally between parallel components, the increase in voltage seen across the parallel combination R<sub>3</sub>//R<sub>4</sub> must also be seen across R<sub>3</sub> and R<sub>4</sub> individually:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table5.jpg" /></p>
<p>The same goes for R<sub>1</sub> and R<sub>2</sub>. The voltage decrease seen across the parallel combination of R<sub>1</sub> and R<sub>2</sub> will be seen across R<sub>1</sub> and R<sub>2</sub> individually:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table10.jpg" /></p>
<p>Applying Ohm&rsquo;s Law vertically to those columns with unchanged (&ldquo;same&rdquo;) resistance values, we can tell what the current will do through those components. Increased voltage across an unchanged resistance leads to increased current. Conversely, decreased voltage across an unchanged resistance leads to decreased current:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table6.jpg" /></p>
<p>Once again we find ourselves in a position where Ohm&rsquo;s Law can&rsquo;t help us: for R<sub>2</sub>, both voltage and resistance have decreased, but without knowing <em>how much</em> each one has changed, we can&rsquo;t use the I=E/R formula to qualitatively determine the resulting change in current. However, we can still apply the rules of series and parallel circuits <em>horizontally</em>. We know that the current through the R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> parallel combination has increased, and we also know that the current through R<sub>1</sub> has decreased.</p>
<p>One of the rules of parallel circuits is that total current is equal to the sum of the individual branch currents. In this case, the current through R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> is equal to the current through R<sub>1</sub> added to the current through R<sub>2</sub>. If current through R<sub>1</sub>//R<sub>2</sub> has increased while current through R<sub>1</sub> has decreased, current through R<sub>2</sub> <em>must</em> have increased:</p>
<p><img src="/uploads/articles/analysis-on-failure-scenario-table11.jpg" /></p>
<p>And with that, our table of qualitative values stands completed. This particular exercise may look laborious due to all the detailed commentary, but the actual process can be performed very quickly with some practice. An important thing to realize here is that the general procedure is little different from quantitative analysis: start with the known values, then proceed to determining total resistance, then total current, then transfer figures of voltage and current as allowed by the rules of series and parallel circuits to the appropriate columns.</p>
<p>A few general rules can be memorized to assist and/or to check your progress when proceeding with such an analysis:</p>
<ul>
<li>For any <em>single</em> component failure (open or shorted), the total resistance will always change in the same direction (either increase or decrease) as the resistance change of the failed component.</li>
<li>When a component fails shorted, its resistance always decreases. Also, the current through it will increase, and the voltage across it <em>may</em> drop. I say &ldquo;may&rdquo; because in some cases it will remain the same (case in point: a simple parallel circuit with an ideal power source).</li>
<li>When a component fails open, its resistance always increases. The current through that component will decrease to zero, because it is an incomplete electrical path (no continuity). This <em>may</em> result in an increase of voltage across it. The same exception stated above applies here as well: in a simple parallel circuit with an ideal voltage source, the voltage across an open-failed component will remain unchanged.</li>
</ul>
