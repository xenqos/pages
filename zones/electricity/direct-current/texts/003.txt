###### 01. Basic Concepts Of Electricity

# What Are Electric Circuits?
<p>You might have been wondering how charges can continuously flow in a uniform direction through wires without the benefit of these hypothetical Sources and Destinations. In order for the Source-and-Destination scheme to work, both would have to have an infinite capacity for charges in order to sustain a continuous flow!</p>
<p>Using the marble-and-tube analogy from the previous page on conductors, insulators, and electron flow, the marble source and marble destination buckets would have to be infinitely large to contain enough marble capacity for a &ldquo;flow&rdquo; of marbles to be sustained.</p>
## What Is a Circuit?
<p>The answer to this paradox is found in the concept of a <em>circuit</em>: a never-ending looped pathway for charge carriers. If we take a wire, or many wires, joined end-to-end, and loop it around so that it forms a continuous pathway, we have the means to support a uniform flow of charge without having to resort to infinite Sources and Destinations:</p>

^[003-01.webp]

<p>Each charge carrier advancing clockwise in this circuit pushes on the one in front of it, which pushes on the one in front of it, and so on, and so on, just like a hula-hoop filled with marbles. Now, we have the capability of supporting a continuous flow of charge indefinitely without the need for infinite supplies and dumps. All we need to maintain this flow is a continuous means of motivation for those charge carriers, which we&rsquo;ll address in the next section of this chapter on voltage and current.</p>
## What Does It Mean When a Circuit Is Broken?
<p>Continuity is just as important in a circuit as it is in a straight piece of wire. Just as in the example with the straight piece of wire between the Source and Destination, any break in this circuit will prevent charge from flowing through it:</p>

^[003-02.webp]

<p>An important principle to realize here is that <em>it doesn&rsquo;t matter where the break occurs</em>. Any discontinuity in the circuit will prevent charge flow throughout the entire circuit. Unless there is a continuous, unbroken loop of conductive material for charge carriers to flow through, a sustained flow simply cannot be maintained.</p>

^[003-03.webp]

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A <em>circuit</em> is an unbroken loop of conductive material that allows charge carriers to flow through continuously without beginning or end.</li>
<li>If a circuit is &ldquo;broken,&rdquo; that means its conductive elements no longer form a complete path, and continuous charge flow cannot occur in it.</li>
<li>The location of a break in a circuit is irrelevant to its inability to sustain continuous charge flow. <em>Any </em>break, <em>anywhere</em> in a circuit prevents the flow of charge carriers throughout the circuit.</li>
</ul>
