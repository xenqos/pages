###### 14. Magnetism and Electromagnetism

# Permeability and Saturation
<p>The nonlinearity of material permeability may be graphed for better understanding. We&rsquo;ll place the quantity of field intensity (H), equal to field force (mmf) divided by the length of the material, on the horizontal axis of the graph. On the vertical axis, we&rsquo;ll place the quantity of flux density (B), equal to total flux divided by the cross-sectional area of the material.</p>
<p>We will use the quantities of field intensity (H) and flux density (B) instead of field force (mmf) and total flux (&Phi;) so that the shape of our graph remains independent of the physical dimensions of our test material. What we&rsquo;re trying to do here is show a mathematical relationship between field force and flux for <em>any</em> chunk of a particular substance, in the same spirit as describing a material&rsquo;s <em>specific resistance</em> in ohm-cmil/ft instead of its actual <em>resistance</em> in ohms.</p>

^[106-01.webp]

<p>This is called the <em>normal magnetization curve</em>, or <em>B-H curve</em>, for any particular material. Notice how the flux density for any of the above materials (cast iron, cast steel, and sheet steel) levels off with increasing amounts of field intensity. This effect is known as <em>saturation</em>. When there is little applied magnetic force (low H), only a few atoms are in alignment, and the rest are easily aligned with additional force.</p>
<p>However, as more flux gets crammed into the same cross-sectional area of a ferromagnetic material, fewer atoms are available within that material to align their electrons with additional force, and so it takes more and more force (H) to get less and less &ldquo;help&rdquo; from the material in creating more flux density (B). To put this in <em>economic</em> terms, we&rsquo;re seeing a case of diminishing returns (B) on our investment (H). Saturation is a phenomenon limited to iron-core electromagnets.</p>
<p>Air-core electromagnets don&rsquo;t saturate, but on the other hand, they don&rsquo;t produce nearly as much magnetic flux as a ferromagnetic core for the same number of wire turns and current.</p>
## Magnetic Hysteresis
<p>Another quirk to confound our analysis of magnetic flux versus force is the phenomenon of magnetic <em>hysteresis</em>. As a general term, <em>hysteresis</em> means a lag between input and output in a system upon a change in direction. Anyone who&rsquo;s ever driven an old automobile with &ldquo;loose&rdquo; steering knows what hysteresis is: to change from turning left to turning right (or vice versa), you have to rotate the steering wheel an additional amount to overcome the built-in &ldquo;lag&rdquo; in the mechanical linkage system between the steering wheel and the front wheels of the car.</p>
<p>In a magnetic system, hysteresis is seen in a ferromagnetic material that tends to stay magnetized after an applied field force has been removed (see &ldquo;retentivity&rdquo; in the first section of this chapter) if the force is reversed in polarity.</p>

<p>Just as in the case of automobile steering systems, hysteresis can be a problem. If you&rsquo;re designing a system to produce precise amounts of magnetic field flux for given amounts of current, hysteresis may hinder this design goal (due to the fact that the amount of flux density would depend on the current and<em> </em>how strongly it was magnetized before!). Similarly, a loose steering system is unacceptable in a race car, where precise, repeatable steering response is a necessity.</p>
<p>Also, having to overcome prior magnetization in an electromagnet can be a waste of energy if the current used to energize the coil is alternating back and forth (AC). The area within the hysteresis curve gives a rough estimate of the amount of this wasted energy.</p>
<p>Other times, magnetic hysteresis is a desirable thing. Such is the case when magnetic materials are used as a means of storing information (computer disks, audio, and videotapes). In these applications, it is desirable to be able to magnetize a speck of iron oxide (ferrite) and rely on that material&rsquo;s retentivity to &ldquo;remember&rdquo; its last magnetized state.</p>
<p>Another productive application for magnetic hysteresis is in filtering high-frequency electromagnetic &ldquo;noise&rdquo; (rapidly alternating surges of voltage) from signal wiring by running those wires through the middle of a ferrite ring. The energy consumed in overcoming the hysteresis of ferrite attenuates the strength of the &ldquo;noise&rdquo; signal. Interestingly enough, the hysteresis curve of ferrite is quite extreme:</p>

<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The permeability of material changes with the amount of magnetic flux forced through it.</li>
<li>The specific relationship of force to flux (field intensity H to flux density B) is graphed in a form called the <em>normal magnetization curve</em>.</li>
<li>It is possible to apply so much magnetic field force to a ferromagnetic material that no more flux can be crammed into it. This condition is known as magnetic <em>saturation</em>.</li>
<li>When the <em>retentivity</em> of a ferromagnetic substance interferes with its re-magnetization in the opposite direction, a condition known as <em>hysteresis</em> occurs.</li>
</ul>
