## 02. Ohm's Law ##

# Ohm&#8217;s Law - How Voltage, Current, and Resistance Relate
<p>The first, and perhaps most important, relationship between current, voltage, and resistance is called Ohm&rsquo;s Law, discovered by Georg Simon Ohm and published in his 1827 paper, The Galvanic Circuit Investigated Mathematically.</p>
## Voltage, Current, and Resistance
<p>An electric circuit is formed when a conductive path is created to allow electric charge to continuously move. This continuous movement of electric charge through the conductors of a circuit is called a <em><a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/basic-requirements-for-current/" target="_blank">current</a></em>, and it is often referred to in terms of &ldquo;flow,&rdquo; just like the flow of a liquid through a hollow pipe.</p>
<p>The force motivating charge carriers to &ldquo;flow&rdquo; in a circuit is called <em>voltage</em>. Voltage is a specific measure of potential energy that is always relative between two points.</p>
<p>When we speak of a certain amount of voltage being present in a circuit, we are referring to the measurement of how much <em>potential</em> energy exists to move charge carriers from one particular point in that circuit to another particular point. Without reference to <em>two </em>particular points, the term &ldquo;voltage&rdquo; has no meaning.</p>
<p>Current tends to move through the conductors with some degree of friction, or opposition to motion. This opposition to motion is more properly called <em>resistance</em>. The amount of current in a circuit depends on the amount of voltage and the amount of resistance in the circuit to oppose current flow.</p>
<p>Just like voltage, resistance is a quantity relative between two points. For this reason, the quantities of voltage and resistance are often stated as being &ldquo;between&rdquo; or &ldquo;across&rdquo; two points in a circuit.</p>

For mass, we might use the units of &ldquo;kilogram&rdquo; or &ldquo;gram.&rdquo;</p>
<p>For temperature, we might use degrees Fahrenheit or degrees Celsius. Here are the standard units of measurement for electrical current, voltage, and resistance:</p>

^[008-01.png]

<p>The &ldquo;symbol&rdquo; given for each quantity is the standard alphabetical letter used to represent that quantity in an algebraic equation. Standardized letters like these are common in the disciplines of physics and engineering and are internationally recognized.</p>
<p>The &ldquo;unit abbreviation&rdquo; for each quantity represents the alphabetical symbol used as a shorthand notation for its particular unit of measurement. And, yes, that strange-looking &ldquo;horseshoe&rdquo; symbol is the capital Greek letter &Omega;, just a character in a <em>foreign</em> alphabet (apologies to any Greek readers here).</p>
<p>Each unit of measurement is named after a famous experimenter in electricity: The <em>amp</em> after the Frenchman Andre M. Ampere, the <em>volt</em> after the Italian Alessandro Volta, and the <em>ohm</em> after the German Georg Simon Ohm.</p>
<p>The mathematical symbol for each quantity is meaningful as well. The &ldquo;R&rdquo; for resistance and the &ldquo;V&rdquo; for voltage are both self-explanatory, whereas &ldquo;I&rdquo; for current seems a bit weird. The &ldquo;I&rdquo; is thought to have been meant to represent &ldquo;Intensity&rdquo; (of charge flow), and the other symbol for voltage, &ldquo;E,&rdquo; stands for &ldquo;Electromotive force.&rdquo; From what research I&rsquo;ve been able to do, there seems to be some dispute over the meaning of &ldquo;I.&rdquo;</p>
<p>The symbols &ldquo;E&rdquo; and &ldquo;V&rdquo; are interchangeable for the most part, although some texts reserve &ldquo;E&rdquo; to represent voltage across a source (such as a battery or generator) and &ldquo;V&rdquo; to represent voltage across anything else.</p>
<p>All of these symbols are expressed using capital letters, except in cases where a quantity (especially voltage or current) is described in terms of a brief period of time (called an &ldquo;instantaneous&rdquo; value). For example, the voltage of a battery, which is stable over a long period, will be symbolized with a capital letter &ldquo;E,&rdquo; while the voltage peak of a lightning strike at the very instant it hits a power line would most likely be symbolized with a lower-case letter &ldquo;e&rdquo; (or lower-case &ldquo;v&rdquo;) to designate that value as being at a single moment in time.</p>
<p>This same lower-case convention holds true for current as well, the lower-case letter &ldquo;i&rdquo; representing current at some instant in time. Most direct-current (DC) measurements, however, being stable over time, will be symbolized with capital letters.</p>
## Coulomb and Electric Charge
<p>One foundational unit of electrical measurement often taught at the beginning of electronics courses but used infrequently afterward, is the unit of the <em>coulomb</em>, which is a measure of electric charge proportional to the number of electrons in an imbalanced state. One coulomb of charge is equal to 6,250,000,000,000,000,000 electrons.</p>
<p>The symbol for electric charge quantity is the capital letter &ldquo;Q,&rdquo; with the unit of coulombs abbreviated by the capital letter &ldquo;C.&rdquo; It so happens that the unit for current flow, the amp, is equal to 1 coulomb of charge passing by a given point in a circuit in 1 second. Cast in these terms, current is the <em>rate of electric charge motion</em> through a conductor.</p>
<p>As stated before, voltage is the measure of <em>potential energy per unit charge</em> available to motivate current flow from one point to another. Before we can precisely define what a &ldquo;volt&rdquo; is, we must understand how to measure this quantity we call &ldquo;potential energy.&rdquo; The general metric unit for energy of any kind is the <em>joule</em>, equal to the amount of work performed by a force of 1 newton exerted through a motion of 1 meter (in the same direction).</p>
<p>In imperial&nbsp;units, this is slightly less than 3/4 pound of force exerted over a distance of 1 foot. Put in common terms, it takes about 1 joule of energy to lift a 3/4 pound weight 1 foot off the ground or to drag something a distance of 1 foot using a parallel pulling force of 3/4 pound. Defined in these scientific terms, 1 volt is equal to 1 joule of electric potential energy per (divided by) 1 coulomb of charge. Thus, a 9-volt battery releases 9 joules of energy for every coulomb of charge moved through a circuit.</p>
<p>These units and symbols for <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/electrical-quantities-a/" target="_blank">electrical quantities</a> will become very important to know as we begin to explore the relationships between them in circuits.</p>
