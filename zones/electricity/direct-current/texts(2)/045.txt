<p>The current through R<sub>1</sub> is still exactly twice that of R<sub>3</sub>, despite the fact that the source voltage has changed. The proportionality between different branch currents is strictly a function of resistance.</p>
<p>Also reminiscent of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/series-circuits-voltage-divider-equation/" target="_blank">voltage dividers</a> is the fact that branch currents are fixed proportions of the total current. <p>Now we can see for ourselves the point we made at the beginning of this page: A parallel circuit is often called a <em>current divider</em> for its ability to proportion&mdash;or divide&mdash;the total current into fractional parts.</p>
## The Current Divider Formula
<p>With a little bit of algebra, we can derive a formula for determining parallel resistor current given nothing more than total current, individual resistance, and total resistance:</p>

<p>The ratio of total resistance to individual resistance is the same ratio as the individual (branch) current to the total current. This is known as the <em>current divider formula</em>, and it is a short-cut method for determining branch currents in a parallel circuit when the total current is known.</p>

## Current Divider Formula vs. Voltage Divider Formula
<p>It is quite easy to confuse these two equations, getting the resistance ratios backward. One way to help remember the proper form is to keep in mind that both ratios in the voltage and current divider equations must be less than one. After all, these are <em>divider</em> equations, not <em>multiplier</em> equations! If the fraction is upside-down, it will provide a ratio greater than one, which is incorrect.</p>
<p>Knowing that total resistance in a series (voltage divider) circuit is always greater than any of the individual resistances, we know that the fraction for that formula must be R<sub>n</sub> over R<sub>Total</sub>. Conversely, knowing that total resistance in a parallel (current divider) circuit is always less than any of the individual resistances, we know that the fraction for that formula must be R<sub>Total</sub> over R<sub>n</sub>.</p>
