# Branch Current Method Analysis
<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Card_Thumbnail.jpg" /></p>
## What is the Branch Current Method Used in Network Analysis?
<p><p>The branch current method is a network analysis technique in which branch current directions are assigned arbitrarily, and then Ohm&rsquo;s law and Kirchhoff&rsquo;s current and voltage laws are applied systematically to solve for the unknown currents and voltages.</p></p>
<p>The most straightforward <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/what-is-network-analysis/" target="_blank">DC network analysis</a> technique is the branch current method. In this method, we assume the directions of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/">currents</a> in the circuit and then write equations describing their relationships to each other through <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/kirchhoffs-current-law-kcl/" target="_blank">Kirchhoff&rsquo;s current law (KCL)</a>, <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-6/kirchhoffs-voltage-law-kvl/">Kirchhoff&rsquo;s voltage law (KVL)</a>, and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/voltage-current-resistance-relate/">Ohm&rsquo;s law</a>. Once we have one equation for every unknown current, we can solve the simultaneous equations and determine all currents and, therefore, all <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drops</a> in the network.</p>

## Selecting a Circuit Node and Branch Current Assignments
<p>To begin with, let&rsquo;s use the circuit in Figure 1 to explain the application of the branch current method.&nbsp;We will use this same example circuit to teach other network analysis methods, including <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/mesh-current-method/">Mesh Current</a>, <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/superposition-theorem/">Superposition Theorem</a>, <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/thevenins-theorem/">Thevenin&rsquo;s Theorem</a>, <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/nortons-theorem/">Norton&rsquo;s Theorem</a>, and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/millmans-theorem/">Millman&rsquo;s Theorem</a>.</p>

### Select&nbsp;a Circuit Node
<p>The first step is to choose a node (junction of wires) in the circuit to use as a point of reference for our unknown currents. We&rsquo;ll choose the node joining the right of R<sub>1</sub>, the top of R<sub>2</sub>, and the left of R<sub>3</sub>,&nbsp;as shown in Figure 2.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_2._Select_a_circuit_node_at_which_you_will_define_the_unknown_currents_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 2. </strong>Select a circuit node at which you will define the unknown currents.</em>

### Assign Currents at the Node
<p>At this node, we will guess which directions the three wire&rsquo;s currents take, labeling the three currents as I<sub>1</sub>, I<sub>2</sub>, and I<sub>3</sub>, respectively, as illustrated in Figure 3.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_3._Define_all_the_unknown_currents_at_the_selected_node_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 3.</strong> Define all the unknown currents at the selected node.</em>

<p>Keep in mind that these directions of current are speculative at this point. Fortunately, if it turns out that any of our guesses were wrong, we will know when we mathematically solve for the currents&mdash;any &ldquo;wrong&rdquo; current directions will show up as negative numbers in our solution.</p>

## Apply&nbsp;Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Current Law at the Selected Node
<p>Kirchhoff&rsquo;s current law tells us that the algebraic sum of currents entering and exiting a node must equal zero. Thus, we can relate these three currents (I<sub>1</sub>, I<sub>2</sub>, and I<sub>3</sub>) to each other in a single equation.&nbsp;</p>
<p>For the sake of convention, let&rsquo;s denote any current entering the node as a positive and any current exiting the node as negative. This situation gives us the following KCL equation:</p>

<p>$$I_1 - I_2 + I_3 = 0$$</p>

## Apply&nbsp;Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law&nbsp;Around the Circuit Loops
<p>Next, we&rsquo;ll use KVL around the loops in the circuit to develop a set of equations that will allow us to solve for unknown equations.</p>

### Step 1: Label All Voltage Drops
<p>Before applying KVL, we will label all of the voltage drop polarities across <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/resistors/" target="_blank">resistors</a> according to the assumed directions of the currents. The polarity is positive where the current enters the resistor and negative where it exits the resistor, as shown in Figure 4.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_4._Label_the_voltage_drops_across_the_resistors_.jpg" /></p>
#### <em><strong>Figure 4.</strong> Label the voltage drops across the resistors.</em>

<p>The polarity or the resistor voltages is consistent with a load that is dropping voltage. The voltage will be higher on the side the current enters the resistor and lower where it exits.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/experiments/chpt-3/series-batteries/" target="_blank">battery polarities</a> remain as they were according to their symbology, with the short-end negative and the long-end positive. Since batteries are sources, the voltage is higher on the side the current exits&mdash;just the opposite of the resistive loads.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Keep in mind that it is okay if the polarity of a resistor&rsquo;s voltage drop doesn&rsquo;t match with the polarity of the nearest battery, so long as the resistor voltage polarity is correctly based on the assumed direction of the current through it. In some cases, we may discover that current will be forced back through a battery, causing this effect (an example of reverse current flow into a battery is when charging a rechargeable battery).</p>
<p>The important thing to remember here is to base all your resistor polarities and subsequent calculations on the directions of currents initially assumed. As stated earlier, if your assumption happens to be incorrect, it will be apparent once the equations have been solved. A negative current and resistance simply mean the current flows opposite to the direction of our initial guess. The magnitude of the solution, however, will still be correct.</p>

### Step 2: Apply&nbsp;Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law (KVL) to the Left Circuit Loop
<p>Kirchhoff&rsquo;s voltage law tells us that the algebraic sum of all voltages in a loop must equal zero. We must tally voltage drops in a loop of the circuit as though we measure with a real voltmeter.</p>
<p>First, let&rsquo;s trace the left loop of this circuit first, starting from the upper-left corner and moving counter-clockwise (the choice of starting points and directions is arbitrary). The sequence of voltage measurements is shown below in Figures 5 through 8.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_5._Measure_the_voltage_across_battery_B1_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 5. </strong>Measure the voltage across battery B1.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_6._Measure_the_voltage_across_the_bottom_wire_segment_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 6. </strong>Measure the voltage across the bottom wire segment.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_7._Measure_the_voltage_across_resistor_R2_(1)_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 7.</strong> Measure the voltage across resistor R<sub>2</sub>.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_8._Measure_the_voltage_across_resistor_R1_(3)_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 8. </strong>Measure the voltage across resistor R<sub>1</sub>.</em>

<p>Having completed our trace of the left loop, we can add these voltages together with a sum of zero as required by KVL:</p>

<p>$$-28 + 0 + V_{R2} + V_{R1} = 0$$</p>

<p>Next, we can eliminate the zero voltage term on the left to simplify this equation:</p>

<p>$$-28 + V_{R2} + V_{R1} = 0$$</p>

<p>As an aside, it should be relatively easy to see that we could have skipped measuring that 0 V section of the loop since it is a single electrical node. This node, by definition, cannot have a voltage drop. However, we left it in our development of the branch current method for consistency.</p>
<p>From here, we don&rsquo;t yet know the voltages across R<sub>1</sub> or R<sub>2</sub>, so we can&rsquo;t insert those values into the equation as numerical figures at this point. However, we do know that all three voltages must algebraically add to zero, thus, the equation is true.&nbsp;</p>

### Step 3: Express the Voltages in Terms of the Unknown Currents
<p>Going a step further, we can now express the unknown voltages as the product of the corresponding unknown currents (I<sub>1</sub> and I<sub>2</sub>) and their respective resistors by following Ohm&rsquo;s law (V = IR):</p>

<p>$$-28 + I_2R_2 + I_1R_1 = 0$$</p>

<p>Since we know what the values of all the resistors are in ohms, we can just substitute those figures into the equation to simplify things a bit:</p>

<p>$$-28 + 2I_2 + 4I_1 = 0$$</p>

<p>You might wonder why we went through all the trouble of manipulating this equation from its initial form (-28 + V<sub>R2</sub> + V<sub>R1</sub>). After all, the last two terms are still unknown, so what advantage is there to expressing them in terms of unknown voltages or as unknown currents (multiplied by resistances)?&nbsp;</p>
<p>Basically, this gets the KVL equation expressed using the same unknown variables as our original KCL equation. To solve for three unknown currents (I<sub>1</sub>, I<sub>2</sub>, and I<sub>3</sub>), we must have three equations relating these three currents (not voltages!) together. This will allow us to use algebraic methods for solving simultaneous equations.</p>

### Step 4: Apply&nbsp;Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law (KVL) to the Right Circuit Loop
<p>Next, let&rsquo;s apply the same steps to the right loop of the circuit, beginning by recording the voltage drops. We&rsquo;ll arbitrarily start at the originally chosen node and move counter-clockwise around the right loop, as illustrated in Figures 9 through 12.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_9._Measure_the_voltage_across_resistor_R2_(1)_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 9.</strong> Measure the voltage across resistor R<sub>2</sub>.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_10._Measure_the_voltage_across_the_bottom_wire_segment_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 10. </strong>Measure the voltage across the bottom wire segment.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_11._Measure_the_voltage_across_battery_B2_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 11.</strong> Measure the voltage across battery B<sub>2</sub>.</em>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_12._Measure_the_voltage_across_resistor_R3_(1)_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 12.</strong> Measure the voltage across resistor R<sub>3</sub>.</em>

<p>After that, let&rsquo;s write out the KVL equation for the voltages around the right loop:</p>

<p>$$-V_{R2} + 0 + 7 - V_{R3} = 0$$</p>

<p>Knowing now that the voltage across each resistor should be expressed as the product of the corresponding current and the (known) resistance of each resistor, we can rewrite the equation as:</p>

<p>$$-I_2R_2 + 7 - I_3R_3 = 0$$</p>

<p>Again, note that we have again dropped the 0 V term.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, let&rsquo;s insert the resistance values in ohms:</p>

<p>$$-2I_2 + 7 - 1I_3 = 0$$</p>

## Solve&nbsp;the Simultaneous Equations for the Unknown Currents
<p>At this point in the process, we have a mathematical system of three equations (one KCL equation and two KVL equations) and three unknowns:</p>

<p>$$I_1 - I_2 + I_3 = 0 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Current Law)}$$</p>

<p>$$-28 + 2I_2 + 4I_1 = 0 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law)}$$</p>

<p>$$2I_2 + 7 - 1I_3 = 0 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law)}$$</p>

<p>For some solution methods (especially any method involving a calculator or computer), it is often helpful to express the equations following two simple rules:</p>
<ul>
<li>Include a coefficient in front of each unknown term, even if the coefficient is 0 or 1</li>
<li>Move all constant values to the right of the equal sign</li>
</ul>
<p>Rewriting the equations following these guidelines we have:</p>

<p>$$1I_1 - 1I_2 + 1I_3 = 0 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Current Law)}$$</p>

<p>$$4I_1 + 2I_2 + 0I_3 = 28 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law)}$$</p>

<p>$$0I_1 + 2I_2 +1I_3 = 7 \text{ (Kirchhoff&rsquo;s Voltage Law)}$$</p>

<p>Using whatever techniques are available to you for solving simultaneous equations, you should arrive at the following solutions for the three unknown current values:</p>

<p>$$I_1 = 5 \text{ A}$$</p>

<p>$$I_2 = 4 \text{ A}$$</p>

<p>$$I_3 = -1 \text{ A}$$</p>

<p>Thus:&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>I<sub>1</sub> is 5 A</li>
<li>I<sub>2</sub> is 4 A</li>
<li>I<sub>3</sub> is a negative 1 A</li>
</ul>
<p><br />
However, what does &ldquo;negative&rdquo; current mean? In this case, it means that our assumed direction for I<sub>3</sub> (into the chosen node) was the opposite of its real direction.</p>

## Redraw the Circuit
<p>Going back to our original circuit, we can redraw the current arrow for I<sub>3</sub> in the correct direction and redraw the polarity of the voltage drop across R<sub>3</sub> to match, as shown in Figure 13.&nbsp;</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_13._Solution_of_our_circuit_currents_and_voltage_drops_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 13.</strong> Solution of our circuit currents and voltage drops.</em>

<p>Notice how current is being pushed backward through the second battery (B<sub>2</sub>) due to the higher voltage of the first battery (B<sub>1</sub>). Even though battery B<sub>2</sub>&lsquo;s polarity is trying to push current towards resistor R<sub>3</sub> in that branch of the circuit, the current is actually being forced back through it due to the superior voltage of battery B<sub>1</sub>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Does this mean that the stronger battery will always &ldquo;win&rdquo; and the weaker battery will always have current forced through it backward? No!&nbsp;</p>
<p>It actually depends on both the batteries&rsquo; relative voltages and the resistor values in the circuit. The only sure way to determine what&rsquo;s going on is to take the time to mathematically analyze the network.</p>

## Calculate the Voltage Drop Across All Resistors
<p>Now that we know the magnitude of all currents in this circuit, we can calculate the voltage drops across all resistors with Ohm&rsquo;s law (V = IR):</p>

<p>$$V_{R1} = I_1R_1 = \text{(5 A)(4 }\Omega) = 20 \text{ V}$$</p>

<p>$$V_{R2} = I_2R_2 = \text{(4 A)(2 }\Omega) = 8 \text{ V}$$</p>

<p>$$V_{R3} = I_3R_3 = \text{(1 A)(1 }\Omega) = 1 \text{ V}$$</p>

## Network Analysis Using SPICE
<p>Finally, let us now analyze this same network of Figure 13 using <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/reference/chpt-7/fundamentals-spice-programming/" target="_blank">SPICE</a> to verify our voltage results. As a side note, we could also analyze current with SPICE, which requires inserting extra voltage sources into the circuit. Besides, we know that if the voltages are all the same and the resistances are the same, the currents must all be the same. In that case, we will opt for the less complex analysis.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Figure 14 is a re-drawing of our circuit, complete with node numbers for SPICE to reference.</p>

<p><img src="/uploads/thumbnails/Figure_14._Adding_SPICE_node_numbers_to_the_circuit_.jpg" /></p>
<em><strong>Figure 14.</strong> Adding SPICE node numbers to the circuit.&nbsp;</em>

<p>Sure enough, the voltage figures all turn out to be the same:&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>20 V across R<sub>1</sub>&mdash;nodes 1 and 2, v(1,2)</li>
<li>8 V across R<sub>2</sub>&mdash;nodes 2 and 0 with 0 implied in v(2)</li>
<li>1 V across R<sub>3</sub>&mdash;nodes 2 and 3, v(2,3)</li>
</ul>
<p>Take note of the signs of all these voltage figures; they&rsquo;re all positive values. SPICE bases its polarities on the order that the nodes are listed. The first node is positive, and the second node is negative.&nbsp;</p>
<p>For example, a result of positive +20 V between nodes 1 and 2 means that node 1 is positive concerning node 2. If the figure had come out negative in the SPICE analysis, we would have known that our actual polarity was backward (node 1 is negative concerning node 2).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Checking the node orders in the SPICE listing, we can see that the polarities all match what we determined through our branch current method of analysis.</p>
