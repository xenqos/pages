# Building Series-Parallel Resistor Circuits
<p>Once again, when building <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/video-lectures/electronic-components-resistors/" target="_blank">battery/resistor</a> circuits, the student or hobbyist is faced with several different modes of construction. Perhaps the most popular is the <em>solderless breadboard</em>: a platform for constructing temporary circuits by plugging components and wires into a grid of interconnected points.</p>
<p>A breadboard appears to be nothing but a plastic frame with hundreds of small holes in it. Underneath each hole, though, is a spring clip which connects to other spring clips beneath other holes. The connection pattern between holes is simple and uniform:</p>

<p>Series - Parallel Circuit Construction on a Terminal Strip</p>
<p>If greater permanence is desired without resorting to soldering or wire-wrapping, one could choose to construct this circuit on a <em>terminal strip</em> (also called a <em>barrier strip</em>, or <em>terminal block</em>). In this method, components and wires are secured by mechanical tension underneath screws or heavy clips attached to small metal bars. The metal bars, in turn, are mounted on a nonconducting body to keep them electrically isolated from each other.</p>
<p>Building a circuit with components secured to a terminal strip isn&rsquo;t as easy as plugging components into a breadboard, principally because the components cannot be physically arranged to resemble the schematic layout. Instead, the builder must understand how to &ldquo;bend&rdquo; the schematic&rsquo;s representation into the real-world layout of the strip. ## Variant Wire Connections for Circuit Construction in a Terminal Strip
<p>An example of a &ldquo;variant&rdquo; wire connection might be the very last wire added (step 11), which I placed between the left terminal of R<sub>2</sub> and the left terminal of R<sub>3</sub>. This last wire completed the parallel connection between R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> in the circuit. However, I could have placed this wire instead between the left terminal of R<sub>2</sub> and the right terminal of R<sub>1</sub>, since the right terminal of R<sub>1</sub> is already connected to the left terminal of R<sub>3</sub> (having been placed there in step 9) and so is electrically common with that one point.</p>
<p>Doing this, though, would have resulted in <em>three</em> wires secured to the right terminal of R<sub>1</sub> instead of two, which is a <em>faux pax</em> in terminal strip etiquette. Would the circuit have worked this way? Certainly! It&rsquo;s just that more than two wires secured at a single terminal makes for a &ldquo;messy&rdquo; connection: one that is aesthetically unpleasant and may place undue stress on the screw terminal.</p>
<p>Another variation would be to reverse the terminal connections for resistor R<sub>7</sub>. As shown in the last diagram, the voltage polarity across R<sub>7</sub> is negative on the left and positive on the right (- , +), whereas all the other resistor polarities are positive on the left and negative on the right (+ , -):</p>
^[051-20.jpg]
<p>While this poses no electrical problem, it might cause confusion for anyone measuring resistor voltage drops with a voltmeter, especially an analog voltmeter which will &ldquo;peg&rdquo; downscale when subjected to a voltage of the wrong polarity. For the sake of consistency, it might be wise to arrange all wire connections so that all resistor voltage drop polarities are the same, like this:</p>
^[051-21.jpg]
<p>Though electrons do not care about such consistency in component layout, people do. This illustrates an important aspect of any engineering endeavor: the human factor. Whenever a design may be modified for easier comprehension and/or easier maintenance&mdash;with no sacrifice of functional performance&mdash;it should be done.</p>
<p><strong>REVIEW:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Circuits built on terminal strips can be difficult to lay out, but when built they are robust enough to be considered permanent, yet easy to modify.</li>
<li>It is bad practice to secure more than two wire ends and/or component leads under a single terminal screw or clip on a terminal strip. Try to arrange connecting wires so as to avoid this condition.</li>
<li>Whenever possible, build your circuits with clarity and ease of understanding in mind. Even though component and wiring layout is usually of little consequence in DC circuit function, it matters significantly for the sake of the person who has to modify or troubleshoot it late r.</li>
</ul>
