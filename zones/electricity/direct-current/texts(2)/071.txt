# What is Network Analysis?
<p>The <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/correct-use-of-ohms-law/" target="_blank">basic application of Ohm&rsquo;s law</a> to combinations of <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/what-are-series-and-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">series and parallel circuits</a> can solve many network problems. However, this page will introduce examples of circuits with multiple power sources or unique component configurations that defy simplification by <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-series-circuits/" target="_blank">series</a> and <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-5/simple-parallel-circuits/" target="_blank">parallel</a> analysis techniques. For these, we will need to rely on network analysis methods.&nbsp;</p>
<p>This page provides an overview of the network analysis methods that are explained in detail on other pages within this chapter.</p>

## Analyzing a Simple Circuit
<p>To illustrate how even a simple circuit can defy analysis by breakdown into series and parallel portions, let&rsquo;s start with the series-parallel circuit shown in Figure 1.</p>

^[071-01.jpg]
#### <em><strong>Figure 1.</strong>&nbsp;A simple series-parallel circuit.</em>

<p>To analyze this circuit, one would first find the equivalent resistance of R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> in parallel, then add R<sub>1</sub> in series to arrive at a total resistance.&nbsp;</p>

<p>$$R_{total} = R_1 + \frac{1}{\frac{1}{R_2}+\frac{1}{R_3}}$$</p>

<p>Then, the total current could be calculated by applying <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/voltage-current-resistance-relate/" target="_blank">Ohm&rsquo;s law</a> (I = V/R) using the voltage of battery B<sub>1</sub> with that total circuit <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/resistance/" target="_blank">resistance</a>. Knowing that total current, we could calculate the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-2/polarity-voltage-drops/" target="_blank">voltage drop</a> across R<sub>1</sub> and then the voltages across <a href="https://eepower.com/resistor-guide/" target="_blank">resistors</a> R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub>. With repeated application of our basic series and parallel circuit concepts, we could relatively easily determine all of the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-1/voltage-current/" target="_blank">currents and voltage</a> drops in this circuit.</p>

## Circuits That&nbsp;Defy&nbsp;Series-parallel Circuit Analysis
<p>As we previously noted, some circuits are more complex than our example in Figure 1 that&nbsp;cannot be solved using simple <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-7/what-is-a-series-parallel-circuit/" target="_blank">series-parallel</a> analysis techniques. With that in mind, let&#39;s look at a few examples.</p>

### Two Battery Circuit
<p>Adding just one more battery to the circuit of Figure 1, as shown in Figure 2, prevents us from using our basic circuit analysis methods.</p>

^[071-02.jpg]
#### <em><strong>Figure 2. </strong>An example of a two-battery&nbsp;circuit that&nbsp;cannot be solved using series-parallel circuit methods.</em>

<p>Resistors R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>3</sub> are no longer in parallel with each other because B<sub>2</sub> has been inserted into the circuit in series with R<sub>3</sub>. Upon closer inspection, it appears there are no two resistors in this circuit directly in series or parallel with each other.&nbsp;</p>
<p>This is the main problem&mdash;in series-parallel analysis, we start off by identifying sets of resistors that are directly in series or parallel with each other and then reduce them to single equivalent resistances. If there are no resistors in a simple series or parallel configuration with each other, then what can we do? The solution will be to use network analysis techniques like the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/superposition-theorem/" target="_blank">superposition theorem</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>This is not the only type of circuit that defies series-parallel analysis. Let&rsquo;s look at another.</p>

### Unbalanced Bridge Circuit
<p>Figure 3 is a <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-8/bridge-circuits/" target="_blank">bridge circuit</a>. If it were balanced with R<sub>1</sub> equal to R<sub>2</sub> and R<sub>4</sub> equal to R<sub>5</sub>, there would be zero current through R<sub>3</sub>, and it could be approached as a series-parallel circuit (R<sub>1</sub>&mdash;R<sub>4</sub> // R<sub>2</sub>&mdash;R<sub>5</sub>).</p>

^[071-03.jpg]
#### <em><strong>Figure 3.</strong>&nbsp;An example bridge circuit that cannot be solved using series-parallel circuit methods.</em>

<p>For the sake of our example, let&#39;s assume&nbsp;that the bridge circuit is not balanced (the ratio R<sub>1</sub>/R<sub>4</sub> is not equal to the ratio R<sub>2</sub>/R<sub>5</sub>). Any current through R<sub>3</sub> makes a series-parallel analysis impossible. R<sub>1</sub> is not in series with R<sub>4</sub> because there&rsquo;s another path for the current to flow, i.e., through R<sub>3</sub>. Neither is R<sub>2</sub> in series with R<sub>5</sub> for the same reason. Likewise, R<sub>1</sub> is not in parallel with R<sub>2</sub> because R<sub>3</sub> is separating its bottom leads. Neither is R<sub>4</sub> in parallel with R<sub>5</sub>. Aaarrggghhhh!</p>
<p>We will need to employ network analysis techniques like the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/mesh-current-method/" target="_blank">mesh current method</a> to solve the <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/solving-unbalanced-wheatstone-bridge-circuits-via-mesh-current-method/" target="_blank">unbalanced bridge circuit</a>.</p>

## Circuits With Multiple Unknown Quantities
<p>Although it might not be apparent at this point, the main problem is the existence of multiple unknown quantities. At least in the series-parallel combination circuit of Figure 1, there was a way to find total resistance and total voltage, leaving total current as a single unknown value to calculate. Then, that current could be used to determine previously unknown variables in the circuit, including voltage drops and individual <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/direct-current/chpt-10/branch-current-method/" target="_blank">branch currents</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>With simple series-parallel circuit problems, only one parameter (variable) is unknown at the most basic level of circuit simplification.</p>
<p>With the two-battery circuit of Figure 2, there is no way to arrive at a value for total resistance because there are two sources of power to provide voltage and current. We would need two &ldquo;total&rdquo; resistances to proceed with any Ohm&rsquo;s law calculations.&nbsp;</p>
<p>With the unbalanced bridge circuit of Figure 3, there is such a thing as total resistance across the battery, which theoretically makes it possible to calculate a total current. However, that total current immediately splits up into unknown proportions at each end of the bridge, so no further Ohm&rsquo;s law calculations for voltage (V = IR) can be carried out.</p>
<p>So, what can we do when facing multiple unknowns in a circuit? The answer is initially found in a mathematical process known as <a href="https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/reference/chpt-4/solving-simultaneous-equations/" target="_blank">simultaneous equations</a> or systems of equations, whereby multiple unknown variables are solved by relating them to each other in multiple equations.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In a scenario with only one unknown (such as every Ohm&rsquo;s law equation we&rsquo;ve dealt with thus far), there only needs to be a single equation to solve for the single unknown:</p>

<p>$$V = I \cdot R$$ where V is unknown, but I and R are known.</p>

<p>$$I = \frac{V}{R}$$ where I is unknown, but V and R are known.</p>

<p>$$R = \frac{V}{I}$$ where R is unknown, but V and I are known.</p>

<p>However, when we&rsquo;re solving for multiple unknown values, we need to have the same number of equations as we have unknowns to reach a solution. There are several methods of solving simultaneous equations, all of which are rather intimidating and all too complex for the explanation in this chapter. However, there are many online resources and scientific and programmable calculators that can solve for simultaneous unknowns.</p>
<p>This is not as scary as it may seem at first. Trust me!</p>
