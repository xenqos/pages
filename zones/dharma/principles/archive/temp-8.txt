The "GUI" vs. The Code

Think of a computer.

    The Code: This is the Upanishads and the Vedas. It is pure math, vibration, and abstract laws of the universe (the "Operating System").

    The Icons: This is Hanuman, Ganesha, and Shiva. In computing, we call this the Graphical User Interface (GUI).

The Rishis knew that 99% of people cannot meditate on an "infinite, formless quantum field" (Brahman). It’s too cold, too abstract. So, they "packaged" complex universal laws into symbols that the human brain could relate to emotionally.
2. Decoding the "Kitsch"

When you strip away the 1970s-style posters, you find that these "gods" are actually multidimensional metaphors.
The Case of Ganesha (The "Elephant")

If you look at Ganesha through the lens of the Darshanas (philosophical schools), he isn't an elephant; he is a representation of the Macrocosm:

    The Large Head: Represents the "Infinite Intellect" or the vastness of the Akasha.

    The Trunk: Represents Viveka (discrimination)—the ability to perform delicate tasks (picking up a needle) and massive tasks (uprooting a tree).

    The Mouse at his feet: Represents the "Ego" or the "Desire" which must be kept small and controlled under the weight of cosmic intelligence.

The Case of Hanuman (The "Monkey")

In the Vedas, the "Monkey" is a classic metaphor for the Human Mind.

    The Nature of a Monkey: It jumps from branch to branch, never still (exactly like our thoughts).

    The Flying/Strength: When the "Monkey Mind" is disciplined and focused on the "Source" (Rama), it gains "Siddhis" (supernatural powers) and can leap across oceans. It’s a story about Mastery of Consciousness, not a zoological claim.
