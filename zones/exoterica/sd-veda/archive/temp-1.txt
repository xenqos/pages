That is the "Million Dollar Question" that sits right at the intersection of physics, philosophy, and faith. You’ve hit on something that even the most hardened skeptics admit is eerie: the Fine-Tuning of the universe.

When you look at these forces, they don’t just exist; they are calibrated with impossible precision. If you tweaked the "knobs" on any of them even slightly, the "magic" would vanish instantly.
The "Fine-Tuning" Argument

Many people share your view because the math suggests that the universe is incredibly "fragile." This is often called the Anthropic Principle.

    If Gravity were slightly stronger: The universe would have collapsed back in on itself long ago.

    If the Strong Nuclear Force were 2% weaker: Protons couldn't bond, atoms wouldn't form, and the periodic table would be a blank sheet of paper.

    If Electromagnetism were different: Chemical bonds wouldn't hold, meaning no DNA and no life.

To many, this looks less like a cosmic accident and more like a meticulous architectural plan.
The Science Perspective: Symmetry and Unity

Science doesn't usually look for a "Who," but it does look for a "How." The current leading theory is that at the very beginning of the universe (the Big Bang), these four forces weren't separate at all.

    The Superforce: Physicists believe that at ultra-high temperatures, all four forces were actually one single unified force.

    Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking: As the universe cooled, this "Superforce" split apart—like water freezing into different shapes of ice—becoming the four distinct forces we see today.

The Philosophical Perspective: "Pure Magic"

You're right that what we call "laws of physics" today would have been called "magic" or "divine intervention" 500 years ago. Even today, science can explain how gravity works (curving spacetime), but it struggles to explain why gravity exists at all or why it has the specific strength it does.

Whether you see a Supreme Being, a Great Architect, or a Mathematical Necessity, the result is the same: we live in a universe that seems "rigged" in favor of existence.

    "The more I study science, the more I believe in God." — Often attributed to Albert Einstein (though his views were complex, he frequently spoke of a "cosmic religious feeling" regarding the harmony of the laws of nature).

Where do we go from here?

There are two main ways to look at this:

    The Design Path: The forces are so perfect they must have been intended.

    The Multiverse Path: There are infinite universes with "bad" settings, and we just happen to be in the one where the "radio" is tuned to the right station.
