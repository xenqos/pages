# Inductor

An inductor is a passive component that stores energy in a **magnetic field** when electric current flows through it. While a capacitor opposes changes in voltage, an inductor **opposes changes in current**.

Its primary purposes include:

**Current Stabilization:** Due to Lenz's Law, an inductor creates a back-electromotive force (EMF) that resists sudden increases or decreases in current. This makes it ideal for smoothing out "ripples" in DC power supplies.

**Frequency Filtering:** Inductors have low impedance at low frequencies and high impedance at high frequencies. They are used as **chokes** to block high-frequency noise while allowing DC or low-frequency signals to pass.

**Energy Storage in Switching:** In buck or boost converters, inductors store energy during the "on" cycle and discharge it to the load during the "off" cycle to regulate voltage levels.

**Inductive Coupling:** They are the core components of transformers and motors, allowing energy transfer through magnetic induction.

In essence, the inductor acts as **electrical inertia**, maintaining a steady flow of current despite fluctuations in the circuit.
